01 Introduction to Triangles

A triangle is the simplest closed polygon, formed by joining three non-collinear points with straight line segments. Every triangle has three sides, three angles, and three vertices, and the sum of its interior angles always equals 180°.

Types Based on Sides

  • Equilateral Triangle — All three sides are equal in length; each interior angle measures exactly 60°.
  • Isosceles Triangle — Exactly two sides are equal; the angles opposite those equal sides are also equal.
  • Scalene Triangle — All three sides have different lengths; all three angles are different.

Types Based on Angles

  • Acute Triangle — All three interior angles are less than 90°.
  • Right Triangle — One angle is exactly 90°. The side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse.
  • Obtuse Triangle — One angle is greater than 90°.

02 Similar Figures

Two figures are called similar when they share the same shape but do not necessarily have the same size. Think of a photograph and its enlargement — the shape is identical, but the scale differs.

Key Idea
Two figures are similar if their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are in the same ratio (proportion).
  • All congruent figures are automatically similar (congruence is a special case of similarity where the scale factor is 1).
  • However, similar figures are not necessarily congruent — they can differ in size.
  • Any two line segments are always similar (lengths are proportional by some factor).
  • Any two circles are always similar (their radii are proportional).
  • Any two squares are always similar (equal angles, proportional sides).
  • Two rectangles are similar only if their length-to-breadth ratios are equal.
Fig 2.1 — Similar figures share shape; corresponding sides stay proportional

03 Similar Triangles

Two triangles are said to be similar when their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are proportional. We write △ABC ~ △DEF to express this.

Definition
△ABC ~ △DEF means: ∠A = ∠D, ∠B = ∠E, ∠C = ∠F, and AB/DE = BC/EF = CA/FD.
Fig 3.1 — △ABC ~ △DEF: same shape, different size, equal corresponding angles

If the areas of two similar triangles happen to be equal, then the triangles are also congruent.

04 Basic Proportionality Theorem (Thales' Theorem)

Theorem (BPT / Thales)
If a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle so that it intersects the other two sides at distinct points, then it divides those two sides in the same ratio.

In triangle ABC, if a line PQ is drawn parallel to BC and it intersects AB at P and AC at Q, then:

Result
AP / PB = AQ / QC
Fig 4.1 — Thales' (Basic Proportionality) Theorem: PQ ∥ BC divides AB and AC in equal ratio

Converse of BPT

The converse is equally important: if a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, then that line must be parallel to the third side.

Converse
If AP/PB = AQ/QC, then PQ ∥ BC.

05 Criteria for Similarity of Triangles

There are three standard tests (criteria) used to establish that two triangles are similar without checking all six measurements:

AA

Angle–Angle (AA)

If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar. (The third angles must also match since angle-sum = 180°.)

SSS

Side–Side–Side (SSS)

If all three pairs of corresponding sides are in the same proportion, the triangles are similar.

SAS

Side–Angle–Side (SAS)

If two pairs of corresponding sides are proportional and the angle between those sides is equal, the triangles are similar.

Fig 5.2 — SSS Similarity: all three pairs of sides in the same ratio

06 Areas of Similar Triangles

When two triangles are similar, there is an elegant relationship between their areas and their corresponding side lengths.

Theorem 2
The ratio of the areas of two similar triangles equals the square of the ratio of their corresponding sides.
Formula
If △ABC ~ △DEF, then:
ar(△ABC) / ar(△DEF) = (AB/DE)² = (BC/EF)² = (CA/FD)²
Fig 6.1 — The area ratio equals the square of the side ratio for similar triangles

This means if two corresponding sides are in the ratio 2:3, their areas are in the ratio 4:9. Remember: if the areas are equal, the triangles are congruent.

07 Pythagorean Theorem

One of the most celebrated results in all of mathematics, the Pythagorean theorem connects the three sides of any right-angled triangle.

Theorem 3 — Pythagoras
In a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of squares of the other two sides.
Formula
If △ABC is right-angled at B:
AC² = AB² + BC²
Fig 7.1 — Pythagorean Theorem: the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of squares on the other two sides

Extended Results — Obtuse Triangles

If △ABC is obtuse-angled at B, and AD is drawn perpendicular to CB (extended), then:

Obtuse Triangle Result
AC² = AB² + BC² + 2·BC·BD
Fig 7.2 — Obtuse triangle: AD perpendicular to extended CB at D

Extended Results — Acute Triangles

If △ABC is acute-angled and AD is perpendicular to BC, then:

Acute Triangle Result
AC² = AB² + BC² – 2·BC·BD

08 Converse of Pythagorean Theorem

The converse is just as powerful: it lets us identify right-angled triangles purely from their side lengths, without measuring any angles.

Converse Theorem
If, in a triangle, the square of the longest side equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides, then the angle opposite the longest side is a right angle (90°).

For example, if a triangle has sides 3, 4, and 5 units: 5² = 3² + 4² → 25 = 9 + 16 ✓. Therefore, the triangle is right-angled at the vertex opposite the side of length 5. This is the famous 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple.

Fig 8.1 — The 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple verifies the converse theorem

09 Key Results & Quick Reference

When two triangles are similar (△ABC ~ △DEF), a whole set of ratios involving their measurements all equal the same value — the scale factor. This is summarised below:

# Property Result
1 Corresponding sides AB/DE = BC/EF = CA/FD = k
2 Perimeters Perimeter(△ABC) / Perimeter(△DEF) = k
3 Corresponding medians Ratio of medians = k
4 Corresponding altitudes Ratio of altitudes = k
5 Areas ar(△ABC) / ar(△DEF) = k²
6 Equal areas If k = 1 (areas equal), triangles are congruent

Important Pythagorean Triples to Remember

  • (3, 4, 5) — Fundamental triple; multiples like (6,8,10) and (9,12,15) also work.
  • (5, 12, 13) — Common in board exam problems.
  • (8, 15, 17) — Verify: 64 + 225 = 289 = 17².
  • (7, 24, 25) — Verify: 49 + 576 = 625 = 25².

Summary Checklist

  • BPT applies when a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle.
  • AA is the most commonly used similarity criterion in proofs.
  • The ratio of areas = (ratio of sides)² — never forget the square!
  • Pythagoras holds for right-angled triangles; the converse identifies them from side lengths.
  • For an obtuse triangle: AC² = AB² + BC² + 2·BC·BD.
  • For an acute triangle: AC² = AB² + BC² – 2·BC·BD.
  • Similar figures: same shape; congruent figures: same shape and same size.
