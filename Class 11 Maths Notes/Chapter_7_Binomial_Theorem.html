Chapter 7: Binomial Theorem

The ultimate guide to expanding binomials: Pascal's Triangle, General Terms, Coefficient Properties, and advanced competitive approximations.

Part 1

The Theorem & Pascal's Triangle

An algebraic expression consisting of exactly two terms is called a binomial expression (e.g., a + b). The Binomial Theorem provides an elegant formula to expand a binomial raised to any positive integer power n.

(x + y)ⁿ = nC0 xⁿ y⁰ + nC1 xⁿ⁻¹ y¹ + nC2 xⁿ⁻² y² + ... + nCn x⁰ yⁿ
  • Number of Terms: The total number of terms in the expansion is exactly n + 1 (one more than the index).
  • Power Rule: In every single term of the expansion, the sum of the indices (powers) of x and y is always exactly equal to n.
  • Symmetry of Coefficients: The binomial coefficients equidistant from the beginning and the end are equal: nCr = nCn-r.
Pascal's Triangle
Each number is the sum of the two numbers directly above it.
Part 2

General & Middle Terms

General Term (Tr+1)

Instead of expanding the whole binomial, we can find any specific term directly using the General Term formula:

Tr+1 = nCr xn-r yr

*For the expansion of (x - y)ⁿ, substitute y with (-y).

Middle Term(s)

The middle term of (x + y)ⁿ depends entirely on whether the index n is even or odd:

• If n is Even: There is exactly 1 middle term at position (n/2 + 1).
• If n is Odd: There are exactly 2 middle terms at positions (n+1)/2 and (n+3)/2.

🎯 Term Independent of 'x'

To find the constant term (the term independent of x) in an expansion, use the General Term formula Tr+1, combine all the powers of x into a single exponent, and equate that exponent to zero. Solve for r.

Part 3

Properties of Binomial Coefficients

For the expansion of (1 + x)ⁿ, the coefficients are denoted purely as C₀, C₁, C₂... Cₙ.

1. Sum of all coefficients:
C₀ + C₁ + C₂ + ... + Cₙ = 2ⁿ

2. Alternating Sum:
C₀ - C₁ + C₂ - C₃ + ... + (-1)ⁿ Cₙ = 0

3. Sum of Even/Odd Coefficients:
C₀ + C₂ + C₄ + ... = C₁ + C₃ + C₅ + ... = 2ⁿ⁻¹

4. Sum of Squares of Coefficients:
C₀² + C₁² + C₂² + ... + Cₙ² = 2nCn = (2n!) / (n!)²
Part 4 (Advanced)

Competitive Topics (JEE Level)

📈 Finding the Numerically Greatest Term (NGT)

To find the term with the highest absolute numerical value in the expansion of (1 + x)ⁿ:

  • Calculate the ratio: m = | (n+1)x / (x+1) |
  • Case 1: If m is an integer, then the terms Tm and Tm+1 are equal in magnitude and are both the numerically greatest terms.
  • Case 2: If m is NOT an integer, let [m] be the integer part of m. The numerically greatest term is T[m]+1.

Binomial Theorem for Any Index (Rational/Negative)

If n is a negative integer or a fraction, the binomial expansion does not terminate (it becomes an infinite series), provided that |x| < 1.

(1 + x)ⁿ = 1 + nx + [n(n-1)/2!]x² + [n(n-1)(n-2)/3!]x³ + ... ∞

Important standard expansions to memorize (where |x| < 1):
• (1 + x)⁻¹ = 1 - x + x² - x³ + x⁴ - ...
• (1 - x)⁻¹ = 1 + x + x² + x³ + x⁴ + ...
• (1 + x)⁻² = 1 - 2x + 3x² - 4x³ + ...
• (1 - x)⁻² = 1 + 2x + 3x² + 4x³ + ...

🔭 Approximation Rule

If |x| is extremely small (x ≪ 1) such that x², x³ and higher powers can be safely neglected, then:

(1 + x)ⁿ ≈ 1 + nx

(This is widely used in physics derivations and error calculations!)
