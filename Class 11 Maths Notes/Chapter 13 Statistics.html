Chapter 13: Statistics

Moving beyond averages: Measuring the spread of data using Mean Deviation, Variance, Standard Deviation, and Coefficient of Variation.

Part 1

Why Central Tendency Isn't Enough

In earlier classes, we learned Measures of Central Tendency (Mean, Median, and Mode). However, these single values do not tell us how the data is scattered. Two entirely different datasets can have the exact same mean. To fully understand data, we need Measures of Dispersion.

The Illusion of Averages
Both datasets share the same center, but their spreads are drastically different.
↔️

Range

The simplest measure of dispersion. It is the difference between the maximum and minimum values of the observations.

Range = Max Value - Min Value

MD

Mean Deviation

The arithmetic mean of the absolute differences of the data points from a measure of central tendency (usually Mean or Median).

Part 2

Mean Deviation Formulas

Because deviations sum to zero around the mean, we use absolute values (| |) to measure the raw distance of each point from the center.

1. For Ungrouped Data:
M.D.(x̄) = [ Σ |xi - x̄| ] / n     (About Mean)
M.D.(M) = [ Σ |xi - M| ] / n     (About Median)

2. For Discrete & Continuous Frequency Distribution:
M.D.(x̄) = [ Σ fi |xi - x̄| ] / N
M.D.(M) = [ Σ fi |xi - M| ] / N

(Where N = Σ fi, and xi are the class marks/midpoints for continuous data)
Part 3

Variance & Standard Deviation

Absolute values are algebraically difficult to manipulate. To solve this, statisticians square the deviations instead. This leads to Variance. The square root of Variance brings the units back to normal, giving us the Standard Deviation (σ).

Standard Deviation (σ)
Measures the typical distance a data point sits from the Mean.
1. Variance (σ²) & S.D. (σ) for Ungrouped Data:
σ² = [ Σ (xi - x̄)² ] / n
Alternate calculation: σ² = [ Σ xi² / n ] - (x̄)²

2. For Discrete / Continuous Grouped Data:
σ² = [ Σ fi(xi - x̄)² ] / N
Alternate calculation: σ² = [ Σ fixi² / N ] - (Σ fixi / N)²

⚡ Shortcut Method (Step-Deviation) for Continuous Data

If classes have equal width h, let yi = (xi - A) / h, where A is the assumed mean.

σ = h × √ [ ( Σ fiyi² / N ) - ( Σ fiyi / N )² ]

Part 4

Coefficient of Variation (C.V.)

To compare the variability or dispersion of two different series (especially if they are in different units, like heights vs. weights), we use a relative, unitless measure called the Coefficient of Variation.

C.V. Formula & Interpretation

C.V. = (σ / x̄) × 100

• A series with a lower C.V. is said to be more consistent, stable, or uniform.
• A series with a higher C.V. is said to be more variable or scattered.
• Note: If two series have the exact same mean, the series with the higher standard deviation is more variable.

Part 5 (Advanced)

Competitive Prep (JEE Level)

📐 Effect of Shift of Origin and Scale

If a new variable yi = axi + b is created from data xi:

  • Mean shifts completely: ȳ = a(x̄) + b
  • Variance scales by square: Var(y) = a² × Var(x)
  • S.D. scales absolutely: σy = |a| × σx
  • Crucial takeaway: Dispersion measures (Variance, S.D., Range) are independent of the change of origin (adding/subtracting 'b'), but they depend on the change of scale (multiplying by 'a').

🔗 Combined Mean and Combined Variance

If two sets of data have sizes n₁, n₂, means x̄₁, x̄₂, and variances σ₁², σ₂²:

1. Combined Mean (x̄c):
x̄c = (n₁x̄₁ + n₂x̄₂) / (n₁ + n₂)

2. Combined Variance (σc²):
Let d₁ = x̄₁ - x̄c, and d₂ = x̄₂ - x̄c.
σc² = [ n₁(σ₁² + d₁²) + n₂(σ₂² + d₂²) ] / (n₁ + n₂)

Variance of the first 'n' Natural Numbers:

For the set {1, 2, 3, ..., n}:
• Mean (x̄) = (n + 1) / 2
• Variance (σ²) = (n² - 1) / 12
