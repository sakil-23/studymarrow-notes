Chapter 11: Intro to 3D Geometry

Elevating coordinate geometry into space: Axes, Octants, Distance, Section Formulas, and advanced Centroids.

Part 1

Coordinate Axes and Planes in Space

To locate a point in three-dimensional space, we need three mutually perpendicular lines intersecting at a common point called the Origin (O). These lines are the X-axis, Y-axis, and Z-axis.

The 3D Coordinate System
Any point P is defined by its perpendicular distances from the three planes.

XY - Plane

Formed by the X and Y axes.
Equation of the XY-plane is z = 0.
Any point on this plane is (x, y, 0).

YZ - Plane

Formed by the Y and Z axes.
Equation of the YZ-plane is x = 0.
Any point on this plane is (0, y, z).

ZX - Plane

Formed by the Z and X axes.
Equation of the ZX-plane is y = 0.
Any point on this plane is (x, 0, z).

The 8 Octants

The three coordinate planes divide the entire space into eight compartments called octants. The sign of the coordinates (x, y, z) determines which octant a point lies in.

Part 2

Distance Formula in 3D

Standard Distance:
The distance between any two points P(x₁, y₁, z₁) and Q(x₂, y₂, z₂) is given by:

PQ = √[ (x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)² + (z₂ - z₁)² ]

Distance from Origin:
The distance of a point P(x, y, z) from the origin O(0, 0, 0) is:

OP = √( x² + y² + z² )

📐 Shortcut: Distance from Coordinate Axes

To find the perpendicular distance of a point P(x, y, z) from the axes:

  • Distance from X-axis = √(y² + z²)
  • Distance from Y-axis = √(x² + z²)
  • Distance from Z-axis = √(x² + y²)
Part 3

Section Formula

Internal Division

The coordinates of the point R which divides the line segment joining the points P(x₁, y₁, z₁) and Q(x₂, y₂, z₂) internally in the ratio m : n are:

( (mx₂ + nx₁)/(m+n) , (my₂ + ny₁)/(m+n) , (mz₂ + nz₁)/(m+n) )

External Division

If point R divides the segment PQ externally in the ratio m : n, simply replace the '+' signs with '-' signs:

( (mx₂ - nx₁)/(m-n) , (my₂ - ny₁)/(m-n) , (mz₂ - nz₁)/(m-n) )

Mid-Point Formula

If R is exactly in the middle (ratio 1:1), the coordinates are:

( (x₁ + x₂)/2 , (y₁ + y₂)/2 , (z₁ + z₂)/2 )

Part 4 (Advanced)

Centroids & Competitive Prep (JEE)

Extending 2D geometry concepts into the 3D plane for complex shapes.

Centroid of a Triangle:
For a triangle with vertices (x₁, y₁, z₁), (x₂, y₂, z₂), and (x₃, y₃, z₃):

G = ( (x₁+x₂+x₃)/3 , (y₁+y₂+y₃)/3 , (z₁+z₂+z₃)/3 )

💠 Centroid of a Tetrahedron

A tetrahedron is a 3D figure with 4 triangular faces and 4 vertices. Let the vertices be (x₁, y₁, z₁), (x₂, y₂, z₂), (x₃, y₃, z₃), and (x₄, y₄, z₄).

The centroid of the tetrahedron is:

G = ( (x₁+x₂+x₃+x₄)/4 , (y₁+y₂+y₃+y₄)/4 , (z₁+z₂+z₃+z₄)/4 )

🎯 Incenter of a 3D Triangle

If the sides of a triangle are a, b, and c (where 'a' is opposite vertex A, 'b' opposite B, etc.), the coordinates of the incenter I are:

I = ( (ax₁+bx₂+cx₃)/(a+b+c) , (ay₁+by₂+cy₃)/(a+b+c) , (az₁+bz₂+cz₃)/(a+b+c) )

Part 5 (Bonus)

Introduction to Direction Cosines

Usually covered heavily in Class 12, but critical for solving advanced Class 11 line problems.

  • If a directed line makes angles α, β, and γ with the positive directions of X, Y, and Z axes respectively, these angles are called the direction angles.
  • The cosines of these angles (cos α, cos β, cos γ) are called Direction Cosines, usually denoted by l, m, n.
  • The Fundamental Identity: l² + m² + n² = 1
  • Direction Ratios (a, b, c): Any three numbers that are proportional to the direction cosines. (i.e., a/l = b/m = c/n).
