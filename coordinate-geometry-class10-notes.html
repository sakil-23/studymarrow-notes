1 Introduction to Coordinate Geometry

Coordinate geometry (also called analytic geometry) is the branch of mathematics that creates a bridge between algebra and geometry. Instead of studying shapes purely through diagrams, we assign numerical values — called coordinates — to every point, and then use algebraic methods to answer geometric questions such as "how far apart are these two points?" or "what is the area of this triangle?"

The French mathematician René Descartes introduced this idea in the 17th century, which is why the system is named the Cartesian coordinate system in his honour.

💡 Why does it matter? Coordinate geometry lets you prove geometric properties using equations and formulas — a powerful technique that is used in physics, engineering, computer graphics, GPS navigation, and more.

In Class 10, the chapter focuses on three core topics:

  • Distance Formula – finding the length between two points
  • Section Formula – locating a point that divides a line segment in a given ratio
  • Area Formula – computing the area of a triangle given its three vertices

2 The Cartesian Coordinate System

A Cartesian plane is formed by two number lines that intersect each other at right angles. The horizontal line is called the x-axis and the vertical line is called the y-axis. Their point of intersection is called the origin, denoted by O(0, 0).

Every point on this plane is uniquely identified by an ordered pair (x, y), where:

  • x (the abscissa) measures the horizontal distance from the y-axis.
  • y (the ordinate) measures the vertical distance from the x-axis.

📝 Note: Points lying on the x-axis have a y-coordinate of 0, e.g. (5, 0). Points lying on the y-axis have an x-coordinate of 0, e.g. (0, −3). The origin O is (0, 0).

3 Distance Formula

The distance formula gives us the length of the straight line segment connecting any two points on the Cartesian plane. It is derived directly from the Pythagorean theorem.

Derivation

Consider two points A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂). Draw a horizontal line from A and a vertical line from B to meet at a right angle at point C. The horizontal leg AC has length |x₂ − x₁| and the vertical leg BC has length |y₂ − y₁|. By Pythagoras:

🔢 Distance Formula
d = AB = √[ (x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)² ]

where A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂) are two points on the plane and d is the distance between them.

Distance from the Origin

If one point is the origin O(0, 0) and the other is P(x, y), the formula simplifies to:

🔢 Distance from Origin
OP = √(x² + y²)
📝 Important: Distance is always a non-negative value. It does not matter which point you call (x₁, y₁) and which you call (x₂, y₂) — the result will be the same.
✏️ Worked Example 1

Problem: Find the distance between points A(2, −3) and B(6, 3).

d = √[(6 − 2)² + (3 − (−3))²] = √[(4)² + (6)²] = √[16 + 36] = √52 = 2√13 ≈ 7.21 units
✏️ Worked Example 2

Problem: Verify that points A(1, 5), B(2, 3), and C(−2, −1) are collinear using the distance formula.

AB = √[(2−1)² + (3−5)²] = √[1 + 4] = √5 BC = √[(−2−2)² + (−1−3)²] = √[16+16] = 4√2 AC = √[(−2−1)² + (−1−5)²] = √[9 + 36] = √45 = 3√5 Check: AB + AC = √5 + 3√5 = 4√5 ≠ BC → Not collinear.

Application: Checking the Type of a Triangle

Using the distance formula, you can determine whether a triangle formed by three given points is:

  • Equilateral – all three sides are equal
  • Isosceles – any two sides are equal
  • Right-angled – satisfies Pythagoras' theorem (hypotenuse² = sum of squares of other two sides)
  • Scalene – all three sides are different

4 Section Formula

The section formula is used to find the coordinates of a point that divides the line segment joining two given points in a specified ratio. The division can be internal or external.

4.1 Internal Division

When point P(x, y) lies between A and B, it divides the segment internally in the ratio m : n.

🔢 Section Formula – Internal Division
P(x, y) = ( (mx₂ + nx₁) / (m + n) , (my₂ + ny₁) / (m + n) )

where P divides the segment AB in the ratio m : n internally.

4.2 External Division

When point P(x, y) lies outside segment AB (on the extension beyond B), it divides the segment externally in the ratio m : n.

🔢 Section Formula – External Division
P(x, y) = ( (mx₂ − nx₁) / (m − n) , (my₂ − ny₁) / (m − n) )

Valid when m ≠ n. Here P lies on the extension of the segment beyond B.

📝 Trick – Finding the Ratio: If you need to find the ratio m : n in which a point P divides AB, substitute the known coordinates into the section formula and let the ratio be k : 1. Solve for k — it's often quicker!
✏️ Worked Example 3

Problem: Find the coordinates of the point that divides the segment joining A(−1, 7) and B(4, −3) in the ratio 2 : 3 internally.

m = 2, n = 3, A(x₁,y₁) = (−1, 7), B(x₂,y₂) = (4, −3) x = (2×4 + 3×(−1)) / (2+3) = (8 − 3) / 5 = 5/5 = 1 y = (2×(−3) + 3×7) / (2+3) = (−6 + 21) / 5 = 15/5 = 3 ∴ P = (1, 3)

5 Midpoint Formula

The midpoint of a line segment is the point that divides it into two equal halves. It is a special case of the section formula where the ratio is 1 : 1.

Substituting m = n = 1 into the internal section formula gives:

🔢 Midpoint Formula
M(x, y) = ( (x₁ + x₂) / 2 , (y₁ + y₂) / 2 )

where M is the midpoint of the segment joining A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂).

✏️ Worked Example 4

Problem: Find the midpoint of the segment joining P(3, 4) and Q(−5, 2).

x = (3 + (−5)) / 2 = −2 / 2 = −1 y = (4 + 2) / 2 = 6 / 2 = 3 ∴ Midpoint M = (−1, 3)

6 Area of a Triangle

When the three vertices of a triangle are given as coordinate points, we can calculate its area using a neat algebraic formula rather than measuring a base and height.

Let the vertices of triangle ABC be A(x₁, y₁), B(x₂, y₂), and C(x₃, y₃).

🔢 Area of a Triangle
Area = ½ |x₁(y₂ − y₃) + x₂(y₃ − y₁) + x₃(y₁ − y₂)|

The absolute value | · | ensures the area is always positive, regardless of the order the vertices are listed.

💡 Memory Tip: Think of it as a "cyclic rotation" – each term is xᵢ × (difference of the y-coordinates of the other two vertices), going around the triangle. Always take the absolute value at the end.
📝 Sign of the Result: If vertices are taken in anti-clockwise order, the expression inside the absolute value is positive; if clockwise, it is negative. The absolute value handles both cases, so either order works.
✏️ Worked Example 5

Problem: Find the area of the triangle with vertices A(4, −6), B(3, −2), and C(5, 2).

Area = ½ |x₁(y₂ − y₃) + x₂(y₃ − y₁) + x₃(y₁ − y₂)| = ½ |4(−2 − 2) + 3(2 − (−6)) + 5(−6 − (−2))| = ½ |4(−4) + 3(8) + 5(−4)| = ½ |−16 + 24 − 20| = ½ |−12| = ½ × 12 = 6 square units

7 Collinearity of Three Points

Three points are said to be collinear if they all lie on the same straight line — in other words, no triangle can be formed by joining them.

There are two ways to check collinearity:

Method 1 – Area of Triangle = 0

If the area of the triangle formed by the three points is zero, the points are collinear. Using the area formula:

🔢 Condition for Collinearity
x₁(y₂ − y₃) + x₂(y₃ − y₁) + x₃(y₁ − y₂) = 0

Method 2 – Distance Check

If three points A, B, C are collinear, then the sum of the two smaller distances equals the largest distance:

🔢 Distance Collinearity Check
AB + BC = AC (or whichever arrangement fits)
✏️ Worked Example 6

Problem: Check whether points A(1, 2), B(2, 4), and C(3, 6) are collinear.

Area = ½ |1(4 − 6) + 2(6 − 2) + 3(2 − 4)| = ½ |1(−2) + 2(4) + 3(−2)| = ½ |−2 + 8 − 6| = ½ |0| = 0 ∴ Area = 0 → Points A, B, C are collinear. ✔

📝 Exam Tips:
  • Always label your points clearly as (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) before substituting.
  • Watch out for negative signs when computing differences — this is the most common error.
  • When finding area, always take the absolute value at the end.
  • For collinearity, the area method is faster than the distance method.
  • The midpoint formula is just the average of the x-coordinates and the average of the y-coordinates.
