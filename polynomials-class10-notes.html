Polynomials – Class 10 Maths

CBSE Class 10 Chapter 2 Algebra NCERT Based

The word "Polynomial" comes from two roots: poly (meaning many) and nomial (meaning term). So, a polynomial simply means an expression with many terms. This chapter builds on what you already know about algebraic expressions and takes it further — introducing zeros, graphs, and a powerful division method.

1. What Is a Polynomial?

A polynomial is an algebraic expression made up of variables and constants, combined using addition, subtraction, and multiplication — where every exponent on any variable must be a whole number (0, 1, 2, 3, …).

Examples
  • 5x³ + 3x + 1 — ✅ Polynomial (all exponents are whole numbers)
  • 2x + 3√x — ❌ Not a polynomial (exponent on x is ½, which is not a whole number)
  • 7 — ✅ Polynomial (a constant polynomial)
  • 1/x + 2 — ❌ Not a polynomial (negative exponent: x⁻¹)

Each part of a polynomial separated by addition or subtraction is called a term. The number multiplied with the variable in any term is called the coefficient.

2. Degree of a Polynomial

The degree of a polynomial in one variable is the highest power of the variable present in the expression.

Examples
  • x² + 2x + 3 → Degree = 2
  • x⁸ + 2x⁶ − 3x + 9 → Degree = 8
  • 4x + 1 → Degree = 1
  • 7 → Degree = 0 (constant polynomial)

4. Zeros of a Polynomial

A zero (also called a root) of a polynomial p(x) is any value of x for which p(x) = 0. In other words, it is the x-value where the graph of the polynomial crosses or touches the x-axis.

Example

For p(x) = x − 3, when x = 3: p(3) = 3 − 3 = 0. So 3 is the zero of this polynomial.

Key fact: A polynomial of degree n has at most n zeros.

6. Relationship Between Zeros and Coefficients

One of the most important results in this chapter is the direct relationship between the zeros of a polynomial and its coefficients. You don't need to find the zeros to know their sum and product!

For a Linear Polynomial: ax + b

Zero of ax + b
Zero (k) = −b/a = −(constant term) / (coefficient of x)

For a Quadratic Polynomial: ax² + bx + c

Let α (alpha) and β (beta) be the two zeros.

Quadratic — Sum and Product of Zeros
Sum of zeros: α + β = −b/a = −(coefficient of x) / (coefficient of x²)
Product of zeros: α × β = c/a = (constant term) / (coefficient of x²)
Quick trick to form a quadratic polynomial from its zeros:
p(x) = k × [x² − (sum of zeros)x + (product of zeros)], where k is any non-zero real number.
Example

For p(x) = 6x² − 7x − 3 with zeros α and β:

  • Sum: α + β = −(−7)/6 = 7/6
  • Product: α × β = −3/6 = −1/2

For a Cubic Polynomial: ax³ + bx² + cx + d

Let α, β, and γ (gamma) be the three zeros.

Cubic — Relationships with Coefficients
Sum of zeros: α + β + γ = −b/a
Sum of products (taken two at a time): αβ + βγ + αγ = c/a
Product of all zeros: α × β × γ = −d/a
Example — Finding a Cubic Polynomial

Find a cubic polynomial whose sum of zeros = 5, sum of products two at a time = −6, product of zeros = −20.

Using p(x) = ax³ + bx² + cx + d with a = 1:

  • b = −5 (since α + β + γ = −b/a → −b = 5)
  • c = −6 (since αβ + βγ + αγ = c/a → c = −6)
  • d = 20 (since αβγ = −d/a → −d = −20 → d = 20)

Answer: p(x) = x³ − 5x² − 6x + 20

7. Factorisation of a Quadratic Polynomial

A quadratic polynomial can be factorised by the method of splitting the middle term. The goal is to express the middle term as the sum of two parts whose product equals the product of the leading coefficient and the constant term.

Example — Split the Middle Term

Factorise: 2x² − 5x + 3

  • Product of first and last coefficients: 2 × 3 = 6
  • We need two numbers that add to −5 and multiply to 6 → (−2) and (−3)
  • Rewrite: 2x² − 2x − 3x + 3
  • Group: 2x(x − 1) − 3(x − 1)
  • Factors: (2x − 3)(x − 1)
  • Zeros: x = 3/2 and x = 1

8. Division Algorithm for Polynomials

Just like we divide ordinary numbers (dividend = divisor × quotient + remainder), we can divide one polynomial by another.

Division Algorithm
p(x) = g(x) × q(x) + r(x)

where:
p(x) = dividend polynomial
g(x) = divisor (g(x) ≠ 0)
q(x) = quotient
r(x) = remainder [r(x) = 0 OR degree of r(x) < degree of g(x)]

How to Perform Polynomial Long Division

  1. Arrange both polynomials in descending order of degree.
  2. Divide the highest-degree term of the dividend by the highest-degree term of the divisor. This gives the first term of the quotient.
  3. Multiply this term with the entire divisor and subtract the result from the dividend.
  4. Bring down the next term and repeat steps 2–3 until the degree of the remainder is less than the degree of the divisor (or the remainder is zero).
Example — Division Algorithm

Divide p(x) = x² − 3x − 10 by g(x) = x + 2.

Using long division: x² − 3x − 10 = (x + 2)(x − 5) + 0

  • Quotient q(x) = x − 5
  • Remainder r(x) = 0

Since remainder = 0, (x + 2) is a factor of x² − 3x − 10 and x = −2 is a zero.

Using the Division Algorithm to find unknown zeros: If you know one zero of a cubic polynomial, divide the polynomial by the corresponding linear factor. The quotient will be a quadratic, which you can then solve easily.

⚡ Quick Revision Checklist

  • A polynomial has whole-number exponents only. 2x + 3√x is NOT a polynomial.
  • Degree = highest exponent of the variable.
  • Linear → degree 1 → at most 1 zero → straight line graph.
  • Quadratic → degree 2 → at most 2 zeros → parabola (U-shape or ∩-shape).
  • Cubic → degree 3 → at most 3 zeros → S-curve graph.
  • For quadratic ax² + bx + c: α + β = −b/a and αβ = c/a.
  • For cubic ax³ + bx² + cx + d: α+β+γ = −b/a, αβ+βγ+αγ = c/a, αβγ = −d/a.
  • Division Algorithm: p(x) = g(x)·q(x) + r(x), where degree(r) < degree(g).
  • If remainder = 0, the divisor is a factor of the dividend.
  • Number of zeros of a polynomial of degree n ≤ n (at most n zeros).
