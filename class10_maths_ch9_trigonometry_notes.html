Some Applications of Trigonometry

Heights, Distances & the Art of Seeing Without Measuring

Angle of Elevation Angle of Depression Line of Sight Heights & Distances 12 Marks Weightage

Trigonometry is one of humanity's oldest mathematical tools — first developed to study the movements of stars and planets. In this chapter, we go beyond ratios and identities to see how trigonometry solves a beautifully practical problem: How do we find the height of a tower, or the distance to a ship, without actually measuring it? The answer lies in angles, right triangles, and a few elegant formulas.

01 — Foundations

Key Definitions

Before tackling problems, we need to be precise about four core terms. Every problem in this chapter is built around these ideas.

Line of Sight

The straight imaginary line drawn from the observer's eye to the object being looked at. It connects the observer (E) to the object (O) directly.

Horizontal Line

A line drawn through the observer's eye that is perfectly parallel to the ground. It serves as the reference baseline from which angles are measured.

Angle of Elevation

When the object is above the observer's eye level, the observer must look upward. The angle formed between the horizontal line and the line of sight (measured upward) is the angle of elevation.

Angle of Depression

When the object is below the observer's eye level, the observer looks downward. The angle formed between the horizontal line and the line of sight (measured downward) is the angle of depression.

03 — Quick Recall

Trigonometric Ratios (Recap)

In a right-angled triangle, with respect to angle θ, the three primary ratios are:

Primary Ratios — Right Triangle
sin θ = Perpendicular (Opposite) / Hypotenuse
cos θ = Base (Adjacent) / Hypotenuse
tan θ = Perpendicular / Base  =  sin θ / cos θ
cosec θ = 1 / sin θ  |  sec θ = 1 / cos θ  |  cot θ = 1 / tan θ

04 — Core Topic

Heights and Distances

The central application of this chapter is calculating heights and distances that cannot be measured directly. This is done by drawing right-angled triangles that model the real-world situation and applying the appropriate trigonometric ratio.

Real-World Uses

🏗️ Civil Engineering

Determining the height of towers, buildings, bridges, and mountains without physically climbing them.

🚢 Navigation & Geography

Finding the distance of a ship from a lighthouse, or measuring the distance of a shore from the sea.

🔭 Astronomy

Calculating distances between celestial bodies such as planets and stars from the Earth.

✈️ Aviation

Determining the altitude of aircraft, or the angle at which a plane needs to descend for landing.

Step-by-Step Problem Solving Method

  1. Read and understand the problem carefully. Identify who is observing, what is being observed, and what information is given (angles, distances, heights).

  2. Draw a neat diagram. Represent the situation as one or more right-angled triangles. Label all known and unknown values clearly.

  3. Mark the angles of elevation or depression. Use variables (like h, d, x) for unknown lengths.

  4. Choose the correct trigonometric ratio — one that relates the known side and the side you need to find. Usually, tan is most useful when you know a horizontal distance and need the height (or vice versa).

  5. Set up and solve the equation. Substitute the known angle's standard value and solve for the unknown.

  6. Check your answer. Make sure the result is dimensionally sensible and answers the exact question asked.

05 — Worked Examples

Solved Problems

Example 1 — Kite Flying

A kite is flying at a height of 60 m above the ground. The string attached to the kite is tied to a point on the ground. The string makes an angle of 60° with the ground. Find the length of the string (assume no slack).

Let: AB = height = 60 m, BC = ground distance, AC = length of string, ∠ACB = 60°

sin 60° = AB / AC  ⟹  (√3/2) = 60 / AC

AC = 60 × (2/√3) = 120/√3 = 40√3 m ≈ 69.28 m

Example 2 — Height of a Flagpole

An observer standing 15 m away from the base of a flagpole measures the angle of elevation to the top as 30°. Find the height of the flagpole.

Let: h = height of flagpole, horizontal distance = 15 m, angle = 30°

tan 30° = h / 15  ⟹  (1/√3) = h / 15

h = 15/√3 = 15√3/3 = 5√3 m ≈ 8.66 m

Example 3 — Height from Angle of Depression

A child at the top of a hill throws a stone into a lake. The stone travels 150 m and the angle of depression from the hilltop to the lake is 30°. Find the height of the hill.

Let: h = height of hill, hypotenuse (stone's path) = 150 m, angle of depression = 30°

sin 30° = h / 150  ⟹  (1/2) = h / 150

h = 150 / 2 = 75 m

Example 4 — Two-angle Problem (Tower Height)

From a point on the ground, the angle of elevation of the top of a tower is 30°. Moving 20 m closer to the base, the angle of elevation becomes 60°. Find the height of the tower.

Let: h = height, x = distance of second point from base

From far point: tan 30° = h/(x+20) → h = (x+20)/√3

From near point: tan 60° = h/x → h = x√3

Setting equal: x√3 = (x+20)/√3 → 3x = x + 20 → 2x = 20 → x = 10

h = 10√3 = 10√3 m ≈ 17.32 m

06 — Exam Strategy

Important Tips & Common Mistakes

✔ Key Points to Remember
  • Unless stated otherwise, always assume the observer's eye is at ground level.
  • The object being observed is always treated as a single point (its top, bottom, or center as the question specifies).
  • The angle of depression from point A to point B equals the angle of elevation from point B to point A — they are alternate interior angles when the horizontal line is parallel to the ground.
  • Always draw a diagram first. Most errors come from misidentifying which sides are opposite, adjacent, or hypotenuse.
  • Pick tan when you know the horizontal distance and want height (or vice versa). Pick sin or cos when the hypotenuse (e.g., rope, string, slope) is involved.
  • Simplify irrational answers like 1/√3 by rationalising: 1/√3 = √3/3.
  • This chapter carries 12 marks in CBSE board exams. Expect one long-answer question (3–4 marks).
07 — Quick Revision

Chapter Summary

At a Glance

Line of Sight

Straight line from observer's eye to the object being observed.

Horizontal Line

Reference line through the eye, parallel to the ground.

Angle of Elevation

Angle above the horizontal line; object is higher than the observer.

Angle of Depression

Angle below the horizontal line; object is lower than the observer.

Key Relation

Angle of depression (from top) = Angle of elevation (from bottom) — alternate angles.

Solving Strategy

Draw diagram → identify right triangle → choose correct trig ratio → solve.

Most Used Ratio

tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent, useful when hypotenuse is not given.

Board Exam Weight

12 marks; 1 expected question. Practice 2-angle problems thoroughly.

Master Formula Summary
tan θ = Height / Horizontal Distance
sin θ = Height / Hypotenuse (e.g. rope, string, slope length)
cos θ = Horizontal Distance / Hypotenuse
∴ h = d · tan θ  |  d = h / tan θ  |  hyp = h / sin θ
