Real Numbers

Revision notes covering all key concepts, theorems, and applications

Part 1

Types of Numbers

The number system is built in layers, each set contained within the next. Here is a clear breakdown of every category you need to know.

ℝ Real Numbers

The complete set that contains every number — both rational and irrational. Every number you deal with in day-to-day mathematics is a real number.

ℚ Rational Numbers

Any number that can be written as a fraction p/q where q ≠ 0 and p, q are integers. Their decimal form either terminates or repeats.

e.g. 3/4, −2, 0.333…
ℚ′ Irrational Numbers

Cannot be expressed as p/q. Their decimal form goes on forever without repeating.

e.g. √2, √3, π
ℤ Integers

All whole numbers — positive, negative, and zero.

…−3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3…
𝕎 Whole Numbers

Natural numbers together with zero.

0, 1, 2, 3, 4, …
ℕ Natural Numbers

The counting numbers — the first set of numbers humans ever used.

1, 2, 3, 4, 5, …
2n Even Numbers

Natural numbers perfectly divisible by 2. Written in the form 2n where n is a natural number.

2, 4, 6, 8, …
2n−1 Odd Numbers

Natural numbers not divisible by 2. Written in the form 2n − 1.

1, 3, 5, 7, …
Quick tip: Odd numbers can also be written as 2n + 1 (where n starts from 0), but the CBSE form 2n − 1 (where n starts from 1) is standard.

Prime vs. Composite Numbers

Prime numbers are natural numbers greater than 1 that have exactly two factors: 1 and themselves. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11.

Composite numbers are natural numbers that have more than two factors — they are divisible by at least one other number besides 1 and themselves. Examples: 4, 6, 9, 15.

Important: The number 1 is neither prime nor composite — it has only one factor (itself), so it belongs to neither category.

  • All Integers are Rational Numbers

Part 2

Euclid's Division Lemma

Theorem

Given any two positive integers a and b, there exist unique integers q (quotient) and r (remainder) such that:

a = b × q + r, where 0 ≤ r < b

Both q and r are unique for a given a and b. The remainder r is always strictly less than b.

Finding HCF Using Euclid's Algorithm

The most important application of the lemma is finding the Highest Common Factor (HCF) of two numbers by repeatedly applying division.

Example: Find the HCF of 56 and 72.

  1. Apply the lemma: divide the larger number by the smaller.
    72 = 56 × 1 + 16
  2. Since the remainder (16) is not zero, replace: new dividend = 56, new divisor = 16.
    56 = 16 × 3 + 8
  3. Remainder is still not zero. Replace again: dividend = 16, divisor = 8.
    16 = 8 × 2 + 0
  4. Remainder is now zero. The divisor at this step is the HCF.

While Euclid's lemma is stated for positive integers, it can be extended to all integers except zero (b ≠ 0).
Part 3

Prime Factorisation & Factor Trees

A factor tree is a visual tool for breaking a composite number down into its prime components step by step. You keep splitting each branch until every leaf is a prime number.

How to Build a Factor Tree

  1. Write the number at the top. Express it as a product of one prime and one composite number.
  2. Keep splitting the composite branches until every branch ends in a prime.
  3. Collect all the primes at the tips — that is your prime factorisation.

Factor Tree for 48

48 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 = 2⁴ × 3

Part 4

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Theorem

Every composite number can be expressed (factorised) as a product of prime numbers, and this factorisation is unique — regardless of the order in which the primes appear.

This is one of the most powerful theorems in mathematics. It tells us that prime numbers are the "building blocks" of all integers.

Algorithm for HCF and LCM using Prime Factorisation

  1. Factorise each number as a product of primes in ascending order, using exponent notation.
  2. For HCF: pick the common prime factors and take the lowest power of each.
  3. For LCM: pick every prime factor that appears and take the highest power of each.
Example: 36 = 2² × 3² and 48 = 2⁴ × 3¹
HCF = 2² × 3¹ = 12
LCM = 2⁴ × 3² = 144
Useful relation: For any two positive integers a and b — HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b
Part 5

Proving Irrationality of Numbers

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic gives us a rigorous way to prove that certain numbers are irrational. Two key rules to remember:

  • The sum or difference of a rational number and an irrational number is always irrational.
  • The product or quotient of a non-zero rational number and an irrational number is always irrational.
Example: To prove √2 is irrational, assume the opposite — that √2 = p/q (fully reduced). Squaring gives 2q² = p², meaning p² is even, so p must be even. Writing p = 2k and substituting leads to q² = 2k², meaning q is also even. This contradicts our assumption that p/q is fully reduced. Therefore √2 is irrational. ✓

Summary

Key Points to Remember

  • Every integer can be expressed as a rational number (e.g., 5 = 5/1).
  • Roots of prime numbers (√2, √3, √5 …) are all irrational.
  • Euclid's Division Lemma: a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b.
  • HCF via Euclid's algorithm: stop when remainder = 0; the last non-zero divisor is the HCF.
  • Every composite number has a unique prime factorisation (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic).
  • Terminating decimal ↔ denominator's prime factorisation has only 2s and 5s.
  • Rational ± Irrational = Irrational; Non-zero Rational × Irrational = Irrational.
