1. Introduction

This chapter explores the measurement of flat shapes that involve circles — focusing on their boundaries and enclosed areas. We look at circles as a whole, then zoom into their parts: sectors (pie-slice shapes) and segments (the region between a chord and its arc). Finally, we tackle composite figures — shapes formed by combining circles with rectangles, triangles, or other polygons — which are very commonly tested in CBSE board examinations.

2. Basic Concepts — Parts of a Circle

Before working with areas, it helps to have a clear picture of the terminology:

  • Radius (r): The straight-line distance from the circle's centre to any point on it.
  • Diameter (d): The longest possible chord; it passes through the centre. d = 2r.
  • Chord: Any straight line joining two points on the circumference.
  • Arc: A continuous portion of the circle's curved boundary.
  • Sector: The closed region enclosed by two radii and the arc connecting their endpoints — think of it as a slice of pie.
  • Segment: The region between a chord and the arc it cuts off — like the piece left after slicing off the triangular tip of a pie slice.
Diagram 1 — Parts of a Circle

2.1 Minor and Major Parts

Every chord divides the circle into two unequal parts (unless it is a diameter). The smaller portion is called the minor part and the larger one the major part:

PartMinorMajor
ArcShorter arc (less than a semicircle)Longer arc (more than a semicircle)
SectorRegion of the smaller arcRegion of the larger arc

3. Circumference and Area of a Circle

3.1 Circumference

The circumference is simply the distance all the way around the circle — its perimeter. If you were to cut the circle and straighten it into a line, the length of that line would be its circumference.

Circumference = 2πr = πd
where r = radius, d = diameter, π ≈ 3.14159 ≈ 22/7

3.2 Area of a Circle

The area of a circle is the total surface enclosed within its boundary.

Area = πr²
Alternatively: Area = (π/4) × d²
Diagram 2 — Circle: Radius, Diameter & Circumference

Example 3.1 — Find the circumference and area of a circle with radius 7 cm. (Use π = 22/7)

Circumference = 2πr = 2 × (22/7) × 7 = 44 cm
Area = πr² = (22/7) × 7² = (22/7) × 49 = 154 cm²

✔ Circumference = 44 cm, Area = 154 cm²

4. Area of a Sector of a Circle

4.1 What is a Sector?

Imagine cutting a circular cake into slices — each slice is a sector. Formally, a sector is the region enclosed by two radii of a circle and the arc between them. The angle between the two radii, measured at the centre, is called the angle of the sector (written as θ).

💡 Special Cases
  • When θ = 360°: the sector is the full circle.
  • When θ = 180°: the sector is a semicircle.
  • When θ = 90°: the sector is a quadrant (one-quarter of the circle).
Diagram 3 — Sector of a Circle

4.2 Formulas for a Sector

Area of Sector = (θ / 360°) × πr²

Length of Arc (l) = (θ / 360°) × 2πr

Perimeter of Sector = 2r + Arc Length = 2r + (θ / 360°) × 2πr

Example 4.1 — A sector has radius 12 cm and a central angle of 60°. Find its area, arc length, and perimeter. (Use π = 3.14)

Area of Sector = (60/360) × 3.14 × 12² = (1/6) × 3.14 × 144 = 75.36 cm²
Arc Length = (60/360) × 2 × 3.14 × 12 = (1/6) × 75.36 = 12.56 cm
Perimeter = 2(12) + 12.56 = 36.56 cm

✔ Area = 75.36 cm², Arc Length = 12.56 cm, Perimeter = 36.56 cm

5. Area of a Segment of a Circle

5.1 What is a Segment?

A segment is the region trapped between a chord and the arc it subtends. Picture cutting a circle with a straight knife — the smaller piece is the minor segment and the larger piece is the major segment.

Diagram 4 — Segment of a Circle

5.2 Formulas for a Segment

Area of Minor Segment = Area of Sector − Area of Triangle OAB
                              = (θ/360°) × πr² − (1/2) × r² × sin θ

Area of Major Segment = πr² − Area of Minor Segment

Area of Triangle OAB = (1/2) × r² × sin θ
📐 sin values for common angles
sin 30° = 1/2  |  sin 45° = √2/2  |  sin 60° = √3/2  |  sin 90° = 1

Example 5.1 — A chord of a circle with radius 10 cm subtends a 90° angle at the centre. Find the area of the minor segment. (Use π = 3.14)

Area of Sector = (90/360) × 3.14 × 10² = (1/4) × 314 = 78.5 cm²
Area of Triangle = (1/2) × 10² × sin 90° = (1/2) × 100 × 1 = 50 cm²
Area of Minor Segment = 78.5 − 50 = 28.5 cm²

✔ Area of Minor Segment = 28.5 cm²

6. Areas of Combinations of Plane Figures

Many real-life objects have shapes that are mixtures of circles and other polygons. To find their areas, we break them down into simpler parts, calculate each part's area, and then add or subtract as needed.

6.1 General Strategy

  1. Carefully identify all the simple shapes that make up the composite figure.
  2. Calculate the area of each component shape independently.
  3. Add areas together (if the shapes are placed side by side) or subtract (if one shape is cut from another).
  4. Keep all measurements in the same unit throughout.

Example 6.1 — Ring (Annulus): Two concentric circles have radii 21 cm and 14 cm. Find the area of the ring between them. (Use π = 22/7)

Area of outer circle = π × 21² = (22/7) × 441 = 1386 cm²
Area of inner circle = π × 14² = (22/7) × 196 = 616 cm²
Area of ring = 1386 − 616 = 770 cm²

✔ Area of ring = 770 cm²

Example 6.2 — Running Track: A track consists of two straight parallel sections of 100 m each, joined by semicircular ends of radius 35 m. Find the total area enclosed. (Use π = 22/7)

Area of rectangular region = length × width = 100 × (2 × 35) = 100 × 70 = 7000 m²
Area of the two semicircles together = πr² = (22/7) × 35² = (22/7) × 1225 = 3850 m²
Total area = 7000 + 3850 = 10,850 m²

✔ Total area = 10,850 m²

8. Key Points to Remember

  • Use π = 22/7 when the radius is a multiple of 7; use π = 3.14 in all other cases — unless the question specifies otherwise.
  • The angle θ in sector and segment formulas must always be in degrees, not radians.
  • A semicircle is just a special sector where θ = 180°.
  • A quadrant is a special sector where θ = 90°, so its area = (1/4)πr².
  • Always calculate: Area of minor segment = Area of sector − Area of triangle (not the other way around).
  • Area of major segment = Total circle area − Area of minor segment.
  • For combination problems, always sketch a diagram before setting up any equation.
  • The perimeter of a segment = chord length + arc length (it does not include the two radii).

9. Practice Questions

Section A — 1 Mark Questions (Very Short Answer)

  1. What is the area of a quadrant of a circle whose circumference is 44 cm? (Use π = 22/7)
  2. If the radius of a circle is doubled, what is the ratio of the new area to the original area?
  3. The arc of a sector of radius 6 cm has length 5π cm. Find the central angle of the sector.
  4. A sector has area 38.5 cm² and radius 7 cm. Find its central angle. (Use π = 22/7)
  5. What is the perimeter of a semicircle of radius 14 cm? (Use π = 22/7)

Section B — 3 Mark Questions (Short Answer)

  1. ABCD is a square of side 14 cm. Four semicircles are drawn taking each side as a diameter. Find the area of the shaded region inside the square but outside the semicircles. (Use π = 22/7)
  2. A chord of a circle with radius 15 cm subtends 60° at the centre. Find the area of the corresponding minor and major segments. (Use π = 3.14, √3 = 1.73)
  3. Two circles touch each other externally. The sum of their areas is 130π cm² and the distance between their centres is 14 cm. Find both radii.
  4. A brooch is made from a circle of diameter 35 mm with 5 diameters drawn in it, dividing it into 10 equal sectors. Find: (i) the total length of wire used, and (ii) the area of each sector.
  5. From each corner of a square of side 4 cm, a quadrant of radius 1 cm is cut out. A circle of diameter 2 cm is also removed from the centre. Find the area of the remaining piece.

Section C — 5 Mark Questions (Long Answer)

  1. A horse is tied at a corner of a square field (side 15 m) with a rope of length 5 m. Find: (i) the grazing area; and (ii) the extra area the horse could graze if the rope were 10 m long instead. (Use π = 3.14)
  2. In a circle of radius 21 cm, an arc subtends 60° at the centre. Find: (i) arc length; (ii) sector area; and (iii) area of the corresponding segment. (Use π = 22/7)
  3. ABCD is a flower bed (a quadrant) with OA = 21 m and OC = 14 m, where O is the centre. Find the area of the flower bed.
  4. A round table cover has six equal designs. If the cover's radius is 28 cm, find the cost of making the designs at ₹0.35 per cm². (Use √3 = 1.7)
  5. A square handkerchief has nine circular designs, each of radius 7 cm, arranged in a 3 × 3 grid. Find the area of the handkerchief not covered by the designs. (Use π = 22/7)

🎯 Study Tips for Board Exam
  1. Write out all formulas every day as a warm-up — repetition builds recall.
  2. Always draw and label a diagram before touching the numbers.
  3. Check whether the question asks for area or perimeter — a very common mix-up.
  4. Use π = 22/7 for multiples of 7 and π = 3.14 for all other cases.
  5. Solve at least 10 combination-figure problems to get comfortable with the approach.
  6. CBSE board papers almost always include one 5-mark question from this chapter — practise those.
