01 What is Trigonometry?

The word trigonometry comes from three Greek words: tri (three), gon (sides), and metron (measure). Literally, it means measurement of three sides. As a branch of mathematics, trigonometry deals with the relationship between the angles and sides of a triangle.

While trigonometry applies broadly to all triangles, the Class 10 syllabus focuses exclusively on right-angled triangles and the acute angles within them. The concepts you learn here form the backbone of more advanced mathematics and are widely used in physics, engineering, architecture, astronomy, and navigation.

Key Idea In a right-angled triangle, if an acute angle is fixed, the ratio of any two sides remains the same regardless of how large or small the triangle is. This consistency is what makes trigonometric ratios so powerful.

02 Right-Angled Triangle Basics

Before defining trigonometric ratios, it is essential to understand how the three sides of a right-angled triangle are named with respect to a chosen acute angle.

Naming the Sides

Important The hypotenuse is always fixed (the side opposite 90°), but the "opposite" and "adjacent" sides change depending on which acute angle you are considering.

03 Trigonometric Ratios

For an acute angle θ in a right-angled triangle, there are six fundamental ratios formed by pairing the three sides. These are called trigonometric ratios.

The Six Trigonometric Ratios

Sine
sin θ = Opposite / Hypotenuse
Cosine
cos θ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
Tangent
tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent
Cosecant
cosec θ = Hypotenuse / Opposite
Secant
sec θ = Hypotenuse / Adjacent
Cotangent
cot θ = Adjacent / Opposite
💡

Memory Trick — "SOH CAH TOA"

Sin = Opposite / Hypotenuse  ·  Cos = Adjacent / Hypotenuse  ·  Tan = Opposite / Adjacent

Worked Example 1

In △ABC, right-angled at B, with hypotenuse AC = 5 cm, base AB = 3 cm, and perpendicular BC = 4 cm. Find sin A, cos A, and tan A.

Solution:

sin A = BC/AC = 4/5
cos A = AB/AC = 3/5
tan A = BC/AB = 4/3

Verify: sin²A + cos²A = (4/5)² + (3/5)² = 16/25 + 9/25 = 1 ✓

04 Reciprocal Relationships

Each of the three primary ratios has a reciprocal. These relationships are critical for simplifying expressions and proving identities.

Reciprocal Pairs
sin θ · cosec θ = 1   →   cosec θ = 1 / sin θ
cos θ · sec θ = 1    →   sec θ = 1 / cos θ
tan θ · cot θ = 1    →   cot θ = 1 / tan θ
Quotient Relations
tan θ = sin θ / cos θ
cot θ = cos θ / sin θ
Note cosec θ is the reciprocal of sin θ (not cos θ). A common mistake is to confuse cosec with cos. Remember: cosec ↔ sin, sec ↔ cos.

05 Trigonometric Values at Specific Angles

The exact values of trigonometric ratios for the standard angles 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90° are derived geometrically and must be memorised.

Derivation for 45°

Consider an isosceles right triangle where both legs are equal (each = a). By Pythagoras' theorem, the hypotenuse becomes a√2. Since the base angles are equal, both are 45°. From this:

45° Values
sin 45° = a / a√2 = 1/√2 = √2/2
cos 45° = a / a√2 = 1/√2 = √2/2
tan 45° = a / a = 1

Derivation for 30° and 60°

Take an equilateral triangle with each side equal to 2a. All angles = 60°. Drop a perpendicular from vertex A to side BC, meeting it at D. This bisects BC, so BD = a. The perpendicular AD = a√3 (by Pythagoras). Triangle ABD now has angles 30° at A, 60° at B, and 90° at D.

30° Values (from △ABD with ∠ABD = 60°)
sin 30° = BD/AB = a/2a = 1/2    |    sin 60° = AD/AB = a√3/2a = √3/2
cos 30° = AD/AB = √3/2      |    cos 60° = BD/AB = a/2a = 1/2
tan 30° = BD/AD = 1/√3      |    tan 60° = AD/BD = a√3/a = √3

Values for 0° and 90°

As angle A approaches 0°, side BC (opposite) shrinks to zero while AC approaches AB. Conversely, as angle A approaches 90°, BC approaches AC and AB shrinks to zero.

Standard Values Table

Angle → 0° 30° 45° 60° 90°
sin 0 1/2 1/√2 √3/2 1
cos 1 √3/2 1/√2 1/2 0
tan 0 1/√3 1 √3 Not defined
cosec Not defined 2 √2 2/√3 1
sec 1 2/√3 √2 2 Not defined
cot Not defined √3 1 1/√3 0
🧠

Trick to Remember sin Values (0° → 90°)

Write: √0/2, √1/2, √2/2, √3/2, √4/2 → which simplifies to 0, 1/2, 1/√2, √3/2, 1. For cos, just read the sin row in reverse!

06 Trigonometric Ratios of Complementary Angles

Two angles are called complementary if their sum equals 90°. In a right-angled triangle, the two acute angles always add up to 90° and are therefore complementary to each other.

If one acute angle is θ, the other is (90° − θ). The following relationships hold:

sin(90° − θ) = cos θ
cos(90° − θ) = sin θ
tan(90° − θ) = cot θ
cot(90° − θ) = tan θ
sec(90° − θ) = cosec θ
cosec(90° − θ) = sec θ
How to Remember Each function and its "co-function" are complementary pairs: sine ↔ cosine, tangent ↔ cotangent, secant ↔ cosecant. The "co" in cosine, cotangent, and cosecant literally refers to "complement."
Worked Example 2

Evaluate: sin 65° / cos 25°

Solution: Note that 65° + 25° = 90°, so 65° and 25° are complementary.

sin 65° = sin(90° − 25°) = cos 25°

∴ sin 65° / cos 25° = cos 25° / cos 25° = 1

07 Trigonometric Identities

A trigonometric identity is an equation involving trigonometric ratios that holds true for every valid value of the angle. Unlike a regular equation, an identity is universally true — it's not "solved" but rather "proved" or "verified."

The Three Fundamental Identities

All three are derived from the Pythagorean theorem. In △ABC right-angled at B:

AB² + BC² = AC² (Pythagoras)

Dividing throughout by AC², AB², and BC² respectively gives the three identities:

Identity 1 — Pythagorean Identity
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

Rearrangements: sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ  |  cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ

Identity 2 — Secant–Tangent Identity
1 + tan²θ = sec²θ

Rearrangement: tan²θ = sec²θ − 1  |  sec²θ − tan²θ = 1

Identity 3 — Cosecant–Cotangent Identity
1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ

Rearrangement: cot²θ = cosec²θ − 1  |  cosec²θ − cot²θ = 1

How to Prove an Identity

To prove a trigonometric identity, always work on one side (usually the more complex side) and transform it step-by-step into the other side using known identities and ratio relationships. Never move terms from one side to the other — that's solving an equation, not proving an identity.

Worked Example 3

Prove that: (1 − sin²A) · sec²A = 1

Proof (taking LHS):

LHS = (1 − sin²A) · sec²A

= cos²A · sec²A    [using sin²A + cos²A = 1]

= cos²A · (1/cos²A)  [since sec A = 1/cos A]

= 1 = RHS  ✓

08 Quick Summary

Here is a concise review of everything covered in this chapter:

All Key Formulas at a Glance
— 6 Ratios: sin, cos, tan, cosec, sec, cot
— sin = Opp/Hyp  |  cos = Adj/Hyp  |  tan = Opp/Adj
— cosec = 1/sin  |  sec = 1/cos  |  cot = 1/tan
— tan = sin/cos  |  cot = cos/sin

— sin²θ + cos²θ = 1
— 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ
— 1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ

— sin(90°−θ) = cosθ  |  cos(90°−θ) = sinθ
— tan(90°−θ) = cotθ  |  cot(90°−θ) = tanθ
— sec(90°−θ) = cosecθ  |  cosec(90°−θ) = secθ
Exam Tips (1) Always start identity proofs from the more complex side. (2) Use the Pythagorean identity to convert between sin and cos. (3) For complementary angle questions, check if the two given angles add up to 90°. (4) Memorise the standard values table — at least one question always tests it directly.
