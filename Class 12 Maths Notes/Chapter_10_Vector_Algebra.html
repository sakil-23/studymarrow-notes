Chapter 10: Vector Algebra

The geometry of physics: Direction Cosines, Dot and Cross Products, Projections, and advanced Triple Products.

Part 1

Scalars, Vectors & Direction Cosines

A scalar is a quantity with only magnitude (e.g., mass, volume, temperature). A vector is a quantity that possesses both magnitude and direction, and obeys the triangle law of addition (e.g., displacement, velocity, force).

Position Vector & Direction Angles
Vector r makes angles α, β, γ with the coordinate axes.

Position Vector

If O is the origin (0,0,0) and P is a point (x,y,z), then the vector OP is called the position vector of P. It is denoted by r.

r = xî + yĵ + zk̂
Magnitude |r| = √(x² + y² + z²)

Direction Cosines (l, m, n)

The cosines of the angles made by a vector with the positive X, Y, and Z axes are called Direction Cosines.

l = cos α, m = cos β, n = cos γ
Important Relation: l² + m² + n² = 1

Direction Ratios (a, b, c):

Any three numbers proportional to the direction cosines are called direction ratios. For a vector r = aî + bĵ + ck̂, the scalar components (a, b, c) act directly as the direction ratios.

To convert Direction Ratios to Direction Cosines:
l = a / √(a²+b²+c²),   m = b / √(a²+b²+c²),   n = c / √(a²+b²+c²)
Part 2

Types of Vectors

Vector Type Definition
Zero (Null) Vector A vector whose initial and terminal points coincide. Magnitude is 0, direction is indeterminate. Denoted by 0.
Unit Vector A vector whose magnitude is exactly 1 unit. Denoted by a cap/hat (â).
â = a / |a|
Coinitial Vectors Two or more vectors having the same initial starting point.
Collinear Vectors Vectors that are parallel to the same line, regardless of their magnitudes and directions. a = λb.
Equal Vectors Vectors having the same magnitude and exactly the same direction.
Part 3

Vector Addition & Section Formula

Triangle Law of Addition
If two sides represent a and b in sequence, the third closing side is a + b.
Section Formula:

If point R divides a line segment joining points P and Q (with position vectors a and b) in the ratio m : n.

• Internal Division: r = (mb + na) / (m + n)
• External Division: r = (mb - na) / (m - n)
• Midpoint: r = (a + b) / 2
Part 4

Scalar (Dot) Product

The dot product of two vectors yields a scalar quantity (a number).

Definition & Properties

a · b = |a| |b| cos θ

• Commutative: a · b = b · a
• Perpendicular Vectors: If a ⊥ b, then θ = 90°, meaning a · b = 0.
• Self Product: a · a = |a|²
• Component Form: If a = a₁î + a₂ĵ + a₃k̂ and b = b₁î + b₂ĵ + b₃k̂, then:
  a · b = a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ + a₃b₃

🔦 Projection of a Vector

The "shadow" or projection of vector a onto vector b is a scalar length given by:

Projection = (a · b) / |b|

*If they ask for the "Vector Projection", multiply this scalar by the unit vector b̂.

Part 5

Vector (Cross) Product

The cross product of two vectors yields a new vector that is strictly perpendicular to BOTH original vectors.

Right-Hand Thumb Rule
a × b is orthogonal to the plane containing a and b.
Definition & Properties:

a × b = (|a| |b| sin θ) n̂
(Where n̂ is a unit vector perpendicular to both a and b).

• Non-Commutative: a × b = -(b × a)
• Parallel Vectors: If a || b, then θ = 0°, meaning a × b = 0.
• Self Product: a × a = 0

Area of a Parallelogram

If a and b represent the adjacent sides of a parallelogram, its area is:

Area = | a × b |

Area of a Triangle

If a and b represent the adjacent sides of a triangle, its area is:

Area = ½ | a × b |

Part 6 (Advanced)

Competitive Topics (JEE Level)

📦 Scalar Triple Product (STP)

Denoted as [a b c], it is mathematically defined as a · (b × c). The result is a scalar number.

  • Geometric Meaning: Represents the Volume of a Parallelepiped whose coterminous edges are vectors a, b, and c.
  • Coplanarity Condition: Three vectors are coplanar (lie in the exact same flat plane) if and only if their STP is zero: [a b c] = 0.
  • Cyclic Property: [a b c] = [b c a] = [c a b]. However, swapping two vectors flips the sign: [a b c] = -[b a c].
  • If any two vectors in the STP are equal or collinear, the STP is exactly 0.

🔄 Vector Triple Product (VTP)

Denoted as a × (b × c). The result is a new vector that lies purely in the plane of b and c.

a × (b × c) = (a · c)b - (a · b)c

Memory Trick: "Back CAB minus BAC" (BAC-CAB rule).

Lagrange's Identity:

A powerful formula connecting the magnitudes of Dot and Cross products:

| a × b |² = |a|² |b|² - (a · b)²
