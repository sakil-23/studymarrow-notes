Chapter 6: Application of Derivatives

Taking calculus to the real world: Rates of change, Tangent geometry, Approximations, and absolute Maxima & Minima.

Part 1

Rate of Change of Quantities

The derivative dy/dx physically represents the instantaneous rate of change of a quantity 'y' with respect to another quantity 'x'.

  • If dy/dx is positive, the quantity y is increasing as x increases.
  • If dy/dx is negative, the quantity y is decreasing as x increases.
  • Parametric Rate: If two variables x and y are varying with respect to time 't', then by the Chain Rule:
    dy/dx = (dy/dt) / (dx/dt) (Provided dx/dt ≠ 0).
Part 2

Increasing & Decreasing Functions

Derivatives act as a slope detector. We can use the sign of the first derivative to determine if a graph is climbing uphill or sliding downhill.

Monotonicity Analysis
The sign of the tangent slope dictates the behavior of the curve.

Strictly Increasing

A function f is strictly increasing on an interval (a, b) if:

f'(x) > 0 for all x ∈ (a, b).

Strictly Decreasing

A function f is strictly decreasing on an interval (a, b) if:

f'(x) < 0 for all x ∈ (a, b).

Part 3

Tangents & Normals

Equations of Lines at a point P(x&sub1;, y&sub1;):

• Slope of Tangent (m): Evaluated as [dy/dx] at (x&sub1;, y&sub1;)
• Equation of Tangent: y - y&sub1; = m (x - x&sub1;)

• Slope of Normal: Since it is perpendicular to the tangent, its slope is -1/m or -(dx/dy)
• Equation of Normal: y - y&sub1; = (-1/m) (x - x&sub1;)

📐 Advanced Geometric Lengths (JEE Core)

If a tangent and normal are drawn to a curve y = f(x) at point P(x, y), their intercepts with the X-axis form specific lengths:

  • Length of Tangent: | y √( 1 + (dx/dy)² ) |
  • Length of Normal: | y √( 1 + (dy/dx)² ) |
  • Length of Subtangent: | y (dx/dy) |
  • Length of Subnormal: | y (dy/dx) |
Part 4

Approximations (Differentials)

We can use derivatives to find approximate values of quantities like √25.3 or (26)1/3 by using the principle of small differentials.

Approximation Formula:

Let Δx be a very small error or change in x. The corresponding change in y is Δy.

f(x + Δx) ≈ f(x) + f'(x) · Δx

*Note: Δy is approximately equal to dy. (dy = f'(x) dx).
Part 5

Maxima and Minima

A point 'c' in the domain of a function where f'(c) = 0 or where f'(c) does not exist is called a Critical Point. These are the candidates for local maxima and minima.

Extrema Geometry
At both local maxima and minima, the tangent is perfectly horizontal (f'(x) = 0).

First Derivative Test

Check the sign of f'(x) slightly to the left and right of the critical point 'c':
• + to - : Local Maximum
• - to + : Local Minimum
• No sign change: Point of Inflection.

Second Derivative Test

Once you find critical point 'c' where f'(c) = 0, plug 'c' into f''(x):
• If f''(c) < 0 → Local Maximum
• If f''(c) > 0 → Local Minimum
• If f''(c) = 0 → Test fails (Use First Test).

Absolute (Global) Maxima & Minima in a Closed Interval

If a function is continuous on a closed interval [a, b], it must attain an absolute maximum and minimum.

  1. Find all critical points in the open interval (a, b).
  2. Calculate the value of f(x) at all critical points.
  3. Calculate the value of f(x) at the endpoints 'a' and 'b'.
  4. The largest value from steps 2 & 3 is the Absolute Max; the smallest is the Absolute Min.
Part 6 (Advanced)

Competitive Topics (JEE Level)

Angle of Intersection of Two Curves:

If two curves y = f(x) and y = g(x) intersect at point P, the angle θ between them is the angle between their tangents at P. Let m&sub1; = f'(x) and m&sub2; = g'(x) evaluated at P.

tan θ = | (m&sub1; - m&sub2;) / (1 + m&sub1;m&sub2;) |

*If m&sub1;m&sub2; = -1, the curves intersect Orthogonally (at 90°).
