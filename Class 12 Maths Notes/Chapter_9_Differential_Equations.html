Chapter 9: Differential Equations

Modeling change: Order & Degree, Variable Separable forms, Integrating Factors, and advanced Orthogonal Trajectories.

Part 1

Order and Degree

An equation involving an independent variable (x), a dependent variable (y), and the differential coefficients of the dependent variable with respect to the independent variable (dy/dx, d²y/dx²) is called a Differential Equation (DE).

Order

The order of a differential equation is the order of the highest derivative present in the equation.

Example: In d³y/dx³ + (dy/dx)⁴ = 0, the highest derivative is the 3rd derivative. Thus, Order = 3.

Degree

The degree is the highest power (exponent) of the highest order derivative, provided the equation is a polynomial equation in derivatives.

Example: In (d²y/dx²)³ + dy/dx = 0, the highest order is 2, and its power is 3. Thus, Degree = 3.

⚠️ When is the Degree "Not Defined"?

If a differential equation cannot be expressed as a polynomial in its derivatives—meaning a derivative is trapped inside a transcendental function—the degree is not defined.

  • Example: d²y/dx² + sin(dy/dx) = 0  →  Degree is Not Defined.
  • Example: edy/dx = x  →  Degree is Not Defined (unless you take log on both sides to free it).
Part 2

Solutions & Family of Curves

Solving a differential equation means finding the original function (curve) that satisfies the equation, effectively removing all derivatives.

Family of Curves
The DE governs the slope field of the entire family.
  • General Solution: A solution containing arbitrary constants. The number of arbitrary constants in the general solution is exactly equal to the order of the differential equation.
  • Particular Solution: A solution obtained by substituting specific numerical values into the arbitrary constants of the general solution (usually given boundary conditions like y(0) = 1).
Part 3

Methods of Solving First Order DEs

1. Variable Separable Method

If the equation can be rearranged such that all terms containing 'x' are on one side with 'dx', and all terms containing 'y' are on the other side with 'dy'.

Standard Form:
f(x) dx = g(y) dy

Solution:
Simply integrate both sides: ∫ f(x) dx = ∫ g(y) dy + C

2. Homogeneous Differential Equations

A differential equation is homogeneous if it can be written in the form dy/dx = f(x, y), where f(x, y) is a homogeneous function of degree zero. (Meaning, if you replace x with λx and y with λy, the λs perfectly cancel out).

The Substitution Algorithm

1. Substitute y = vx.
2. Differentiate with respect to x: dy/dx = v + x(dv/dx).
3. Substitute both back into the original equation. The 'x' terms will cancel out, reducing the problem to a simple Variable Separable equation in terms of 'v' and 'x'.
4. Solve, then replace 'v' with 'y/x' at the very end.

*Note: If the equation is in the form dx/dy, substitute x = vy instead.

3. Linear Differential Equations (LDE)

One of the most important patterns in calculus. An equation is linear if the dependent variable and its derivative appear only in the first degree and are not multiplied together.

The Integrating Factor (I.F.) Pipeline
Property Standard LDE (y as dependent) Reverse LDE (x as dependent)
Format dy/dx + Py = Q dx/dy + P₁x = Q₁
P and Q are... Functions of x (or constants) Functions of y (or constants)
Integrating Factor e∫ P dx e∫ P₁ dy
General Solution y · (I.F.) = ∫ Q · (I.F.) dx + C x · (I.F.) = ∫ Q₁ · (I.F.) dy + C
Part 4 (Advanced)

Competitive Topics (JEE Level)

🔥 Equations Reducible to LDE (Bernoulli's Equation)

If the equation takes the form dy/dx + Py = Q · yn (where n ≠ 0, 1), it is not linear. However, it can be reduced to a Linear Differential Equation.

  1. Divide the entire equation by yn.
  2. Substitute v = y1-n.
  3. The equation will magically transform into a standard Linear Differential Equation in terms of 'v' and 'x'.

📐 Orthogonal Trajectories

An orthogonal trajectory is a curve that intersects every curve of a given family at exactly 90 degrees (right angles).

Algorithm:

  1. Form the differential equation for the given family of curves (eliminate arbitrary constants). Let's call it dy/dx = f(x,y).
  2. Because perpendicular slopes multiply to -1 (m₁m₂ = -1), replace dy/dx with -dx/dy in the equation.
  3. Solve this new differential equation. The resulting family of curves is the orthogonal trajectory.
Orthogonal Trajectories
The family of straight lines (y=mx) is the orthogonal trajectory to the family of concentric circles.
