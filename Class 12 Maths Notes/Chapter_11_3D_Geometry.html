Chapter 11: Three-Dimensional Geometry

Applying vector algebra to real space: Lines, Planes, Shortest Distances, and competitive geometric projections.

Part 1

Direction Cosines & Ratios of a Line

A line in 3D space extends infinitely in two opposite directions. If a directed line makes angles α, β, and γ with the positive coordinate axes, then l = cos α, m = cos β, n = cos γ are its direction cosines.

  • Reversal: If we reverse the direction of the line, the angles become π-α, π-β, π-γ. Thus, the new direction cosines are -l, -m, -n.
  • Line through 2 Points: For a line passing through P(x₁, y₁, z₁) and Q(x₂, y₂, z₂), the direction ratios (DRs) are proportional to (x₂ - x₁, y₂ - y₁, z₂ - z₁).
  • Perpendicular Lines: Two lines with DRs (a₁, b₁, c₁) and (a₂, b₂, c₂) are perpendicular if a₁a₂ + b₁b₂ + c₁c₂ = 0.
  • Parallel Lines: Two lines are parallel if their DRs are proportional: a₁/a₂ = b₁/b₂ = c₁/c₂.
Part 2

Equation of a Line in Space

Line through A, parallel to b
The variable position vector r reaches any point P on the line.

1. Point and Direction

Line passing through point with position vector a and parallel to vector b.

• Vector Form: r = a + λb
• Cartesian Form:
(x - x₁)/a = (y - y₁)/b = (z - z₁)/c

2. Passing through 2 Points

Line passing through two points with position vectors a and b.

• Vector Form: r = a + λ(b - a)
• Cartesian Form:
(x - x₁)/(x₂ - x₁) = (y - y₁)/(y₂ - y₁) = (z - z₁)/(z₂ - z₁)

Shortest Distance Between Skew Lines

In 3D space, two lines can be non-parallel and non-intersecting. Such lines are called Skew Lines. The shortest distance between them is the common perpendicular segment to both lines.

Skew Lines
Lines that lie in different parallel planes.
1. Distance between Skew Lines:
Let lines be r = a₁ + λb₁ and r = a₂ + μb₂.

d = | (b₁ × b₂) · (a₂ - a₁) | / | b₁ × b₂ |

*If this distance d = 0, the lines intersect (they are coplanar).
2. Distance between Parallel Lines:
If the lines are parallel, b₁ = b₂ = b.

d = | b × (a₂ - a₁) | / | b |
Part 3

Equation of a Plane

A plane is uniquely determined if any one of the following is given:

1. Normal Form

Equation of a plane at distance 'd' from the origin, with normal unit vector n̂:

• Vector: r · n̂ = d
• Cartesian: lx + my + nz = d

2. Point and Normal

Plane passing through point a and perpendicular to vector n:

• Vector: (r - a) · n = 0
• Cartesian: A(x-x₁) + B(y-y₁) + C(z-z₁) = 0

3. Passing through Three Non-Collinear Points:
For points A(a), B(b), C(c):
(r - a) · [(b - a) × (c - a)] = 0

4. Intercept Form:
If a plane makes intercepts a, b, c on the x, y, and z axes respectively:
x/a + y/b + z/c = 1

Part 5 (Advanced)

Competitive Shortcuts (JEE Level)

🎯 Foot of Perpendicular & Image of a Point

Let point P be (x₁, y₁, z₁) and the plane be Ax + By + Cz + D = 0. We want to find the Foot (x,y,z) or the Image (x',y',z').

  • For Foot of Perpendicular:
    (x-x₁)/A = (y-y₁)/B = (z-z₁)/C = - (Ax₁+By₁+Cz₁+D) / (A²+B²+C²)
  • For Image (Reflection):
    (x'-x₁)/A = (y'-y₁)/B = (z'-z₁)/C = - 2(Ax₁+By₁+Cz₁+D) / (A²+B²+C²)
Image in a Plane
The plane acts as a perfect mirror bisecting PP'.

🔗 Family of Planes

The equation of ANY plane passing through the line of intersection of two given planes P₁ = 0 and P₂ = 0 is written as:

P₁ + λP₂ = 0

(λ is found using an extra given condition, such as passing through a 3rd point or being perpendicular to another plane).
