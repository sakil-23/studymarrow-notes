Chapter 5: Continuity & Differentiability

Mastering the geometry of calculus: Continuous curves, smooth derivatives, sharp corners, and Mean Value Theorems.

Part 1

Concept of Continuity

Geometrically, a function is continuous at a point if its graph has no breaks, jumps, or holes at that point. You should be able to draw the graph through that point without lifting your pen.

Continuous at x = a
LHL = RHL = f(a)
Discontinuous at x = a
Jump Discontinuity: LHL ≠ RHL
The Master Condition for Continuity:

A function f(x) is continuous at x = a if and only if:
limx→a⁻ f(x) = limx→a⁺ f(x) = f(a)

(Left Hand Limit = Right Hand Limit = Value of the function at 'a')

Algebra of Continuous Functions

If f and g are two continuous functions at x = c, then:
• f + g is continuous.
• f - g is continuous.
• f × g is continuous.
• f / g is continuous (provided g(c) ≠ 0).

Part 2

Differentiability & Sharp Corners

Geometrically, a function is differentiable at a point if it has a unique, non-vertical tangent line at that point. The curve must be "smooth".

Condition for Differentiability:

A function is differentiable at x = a if Left Hand Derivative (LHD) equals Right Hand Derivative (RHD).

• LHD: limh→0 [ f(a - h) - f(a) ] / -h
• RHD: limh→0 [ f(a + h) - f(a) ] / h

If LHD = RHD, the derivative exists and is denoted as f'(a).

⚠️ The Golden Rule of Calculus

Every differentiable function is continuous, BUT every continuous function is NOT necessarily differentiable.

The classic example is the Modulus function f(x) = |x| at x = 0. It is continuous (no break), but it forms a "sharp corner" or V-shape at the origin. Because you can draw infinite tangents at that sharp point, it is NOT differentiable at x = 0.

Sharp Corner at Origin
Continuous, but NOT Differentiable.
Part 3

Differentiation Techniques

1. The Chain Rule

Used for composite functions like f(g(x)). Differentiate the outside function, then multiply by the derivative of the inside function.

d/dx [ f(g(x)) ] = f'(g(x)) × g'(x)

2. Implicit Differentiation

When variables x and y are mixed up (e.g., x² + y² = sin(xy)), differentiate both sides with respect to x. Remember that differentiating 'y' requires multiplying by dy/dx due to the chain rule.

💡 3. Logarithmic Differentiation

Used specifically when you have a variable raised to a variable power, such as y = u(x)v(x), or a massive product/quotient of many functions.

  1. Take the natural logarithm (loge or ln) on both sides:
    log(y) = v(x) × log(u(x))
  2. Differentiate implicitly:
    (1/y) × dy/dx = [Product Rule applied to RHS]
  3. Multiply 'y' to the other side to isolate dy/dx.

4. Parametric Differentiation

When x and y are defined separately in terms of a third variable, say 't' (e.g., x = f(t), y = g(t)).

First find dx/dt and dy/dt separately. Then divide them:
dy/dx = (dy/dt) / (dx/dt)

Part 4

Second Order Derivatives

If y = f(x), the first derivative is dy/dx. If we differentiate dy/dx again with respect to x, we get the second order derivative.

Notation:

d/dx [ dy/dx ] = d²y / dx²   or   y''   or   f''(x)   or   y₂

Caution for Parametric Equations: To find d²y/dx² for parametric equations, you CANNOT simply divide d²y/dt² by d²x/dt². You must differentiate dy/dx with respect to 't', and then multiply by dt/dx!
Part 5 (Advanced)

Rolle's & Mean Value Theorems

Lagrange's Mean Value Theorem (MVT)
There exists at least one tangent parallel to the secant line AB.

Rolle's Theorem

If a function f(x) is:
1. Continuous in [a, b]
2. Differentiable in (a, b)
3. f(a) = f(b)

Then there exists at least one point c ∈ (a, b) such that the slope is strictly zero: f'(c) = 0.

Lagrange's MVT

If a function f(x) is:
1. Continuous in [a, b]
2. Differentiable in (a, b)

Then there exists at least one point c ∈ (a, b) where the tangent is parallel to the secant AB:
f'(c) = [ f(b) - f(a) ] / (b - a)
