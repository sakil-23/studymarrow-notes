Chapter 8: Application of Integrals

Transforming calculus into geometry: Area under curves, bounded regions, symmetry mapping, and advanced competitive area shortcuts.

Part 1

Curve Tracing & Symmetry

Before finding the area, you must know what the curve looks like. Identifying symmetry drastically reduces calculation time because you can integrate one quadrant and multiply by the number of symmetrical regions.

Symmetry about X-axis

If the equation of the curve remains unchanged when 'y' is replaced by '-y' (i.e., all powers of y are even).
Example: y² = 4ax

Symmetry about Y-axis

If the equation of the curve remains unchanged when 'x' is replaced by '-x' (i.e., all powers of x are even).
Example: x² = 4ay

Symmetry in Opposite Quadrants

If the equation remains unchanged when 'x' and 'y' are replaced by '-x' and '-y' simultaneously. (Symmetry about the origin).
Example: xy = c²

Part 2

Area Under Simple Curves

We use the method of slicing the area into infinitely thin rectangular strips. We can slice vertically (with respect to x) or horizontally (with respect to y).

Integration along X-axis
Using vertical strips of width dx and height y.
1. Area bounded by X-axis:

If the region is bounded by the curve y = f(x), the x-axis, and the ordinates x = a, x = b:
Area = ∫ab |y| dx = ∫ab |f(x)| dx

*Note: The absolute value |y| is crucial because area cannot be negative. If the curve dips below the x-axis, the integral evaluates to a negative number, but the physical area is positive.
2. Area bounded by Y-axis:

If the region is bounded by the curve x = g(y), the y-axis, and the abscissae y = c, y = d:
Area = ∫cd |x| dy = ∫cd |g(y)| dy

*We use horizontal strips of width 'dy' and length 'x'.
Part 3

Area Bounded by Two Curves

To find the area trapped completely between two intersecting curves, we integrate the difference between the upper curve and the lower curve.

Area Between Two Curves
Area = Integral of (Upper Curve - Lower Curve)
Algorithm for Two Curves:

1. Solve the equations of the two curves simultaneously to find their points of intersection. These x-coordinates become your limits 'a' and 'b'.
2. Determine which curve acts as the "ceiling" (Upper) and which acts as the "floor" (Lower) between x=a and x=b.
3. Apply the formula:
Area = ∫ab [ f(x) - g(x) ] dx
Part 4

Standard Shapes & Area Equations

Geometric Shape Standard Equation Setup for Integration
Circle x² + y² = a² y = ±√(a² - x²). Total Area = 4 × ∫0a √(a² - x²) dx
Ellipse x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 y = ±(b/a)√(a² - x²). Total Area = 4 × ∫0a (b/a)√(a² - x²) dx
Parabola (Right) y² = 4ax y = ±2√(ax)
Parabola (Up) x² = 4ay y = x² / 4a
Part 5 (Advanced)

JEE Shortcuts (Direct Formulas)

Competitive exams test your speed. If you recognize these standard bounded region setups, you can completely bypass integration and apply these direct geometric formulas.

Two Standard Parabolas
y² = 4ax & x² = 4by
Parabola and Line
y² = 4ax & y = mx

🔥 The "Cheat Code" Formulas

  • Total Area of an Ellipse (x²/a² + y²/b² = 1):
    Area = πab
  • Area bounded by y² = 4ax and x² = 4by:
    Area = (16ab) / 3
  • Area bounded by y² = 4ax and line y = mx:
    Area = (8a²) / (3m³)
  • Area bounded by y² = 4ax and its Latus Rectum (x = a):
    Area = (8a²) / 3
