Moving beyond basic chance: Conditional Probability, Independent Events, Bayes' Theorem, and Random Variables.

Part 1

Conditional Probability

In Class 11, we dealt with single, isolated events. In Class 12, we ask: "What is the probability of event A happening, given that event B has already occurred?" This is called Conditional Probability.

Visualizing P(A|B)
Event B becomes the new "Sample Space". We only care about the part of A that lives inside B.
Formula for Conditional Probability:

The probability of event A given that B has occurred is denoted by P(A|B).

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)     (Provided P(B) ≠ 0)

Similarly, P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)

Multiplication Theorem

By simply cross-multiplying the conditional probability formula, we can find the probability of two events happening together (intersection):

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
P(A ∩ B) = P(B) × P(A|B)

Properties

• P(S|B) = P(B|B) = 1
• P(A'|B) = 1 - P(A|B)
• P((A ∪ B)|C) = P(A|C) + P(B|C) - P((A ∩ B)|C)

Part 2

Independent Events

Two events are independent if the occurrence (or non-occurrence) of one does absolutely nothing to affect the probability of the other.

Mathematical Definition:

If A and B are independent events, then P(A|B) is just P(A). Substituting this into the Multiplication Theorem gives the golden rule of independence:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

⚠️ Independent vs. Mutually Exclusive (JEE Core Trap)

Do not confuse these two concepts! They are entirely different.

  • Mutually Exclusive: Events cannot happen at the same time.
    P(A ∩ B) = 0. (Sets do not overlap).
  • Independent: Events can happen at the same time, but they don't influence each other.
    P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B).
  • Crucial Fact: Two non-zero events CANNOT be both mutually exclusive and independent simultaneously.
Part 3

Total Probability & Bayes' Theorem

Partition of Sample Space
Event A draws probability from E&sub1;, E&sub2;, and E&sub3;.

Theorem of Total Probability

If E&sub1;, E&sub2;, ..., En form a set of mutually exclusive and exhaustive events (a partition), and A is any event that occurs with them, the total probability of A is the sum of all the "paths" leading to A:

P(A) = P(E&sub1;)P(A|E&sub1;) + P(E&sub2;)P(A|E&sub2;) + ... + P(En)P(A|En)

🎯 Bayes' Theorem (Reverse Probability)

While Total Probability asks "What is the chance of A happening?", Bayes' Theorem asks: "Given that A has ALREADY happened, what is the probability it came from source E&sub1;?"

P(Ei | A) = [ P(Ei) × P(A | Ei) ] / [ Σ P(Ej) × P(A | Ej) ]

In simple words: (Favorable Path to A) / (Sum of All Paths to A).

Part 4

Random Variables & Distributions

A random variable (usually denoted by X) is a real-valued function whose domain is the sample space of a random experiment. It assigns a number to every outcome.

X (Value) x&sub1; x&sub2; ... xn
P(X) (Probability) p&sub1; p&sub2; ... pn

Condition: pi ≥ 0 and Σ pi = 1.

Mean and Variance:

• Mean (Expected Value, μ): E(X) = Σ xi pi
• Variance (σ²): Var(X) = E(X²) - [E(X)]²
  (Where E(X²) = Σ xi² pi)
• Standard Deviation (σ): √Var(X)
Part 5

Bernoulli Trials & Binomial Distribution

Bernoulli Trials

Trials of a random experiment are Bernoulli trials if they satisfy 4 conditions:
1. Finite number of trials.
2. Trials are mutually independent.
3. Exactly two outcomes: Success (p) or Failure (q).
4. Probability of success (p) remains constant in each trial.

🎲 The Binomial Distribution Formula

If an experiment consists of 'n' Bernoulli trials, with probability of success 'p' and probability of failure 'q' (where q = 1 - p), then the probability of getting exactly 'r' successes is:

P(X = r) = nCr × pr × qn - r

  • Mean (μ): np
  • Variance (σ²): npq
  • Standard Deviation (σ): √(npq)
Binomial Distribution Curve
The probabilities form a discrete, bell-shaped distribution peaking near the Mean (np).
