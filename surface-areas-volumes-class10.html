Section 01

Introduction

Surface Areas and Volumes is one of the most scoring and frequently tested chapters in CBSE Class 10 Mathematics. Board exam papers almost always carry at least one 5-mark question and one 3-mark question from this chapter. Building on your earlier knowledge of three-dimensional shapes, this chapter introduces combinations of solids — figures formed by joining or hollowing out two or more basic shapes.

In everyday life, these concepts appear constantly — from designing water tanks, tents, and containers to figuring out how much material is required to manufacture a product. Mastering this chapter strengthens your foundation for competitive exams such as JEE and NEET as well.

2.1 Cuboid

A cuboid is a box-shaped three-dimensional figure with six rectangular faces, twelve edges, and eight vertices, with all angles equal to 90°. A cube is simply a special case where all three dimensions are equal.

Cuboid Formulas
TSA = 2(lb + bh + lh)
LSA = 2(l + b) × h
Volume = l × b × h
Diagonal = √(l² + b² + h²)
where l = length, b = breadth, h = height

The Lateral Surface Area is useful when you only need the area of the four side walls — for instance, when painting a room without counting the floor and ceiling.

2.2 Cylinder

A cylinder consists of two identical circular bases connected by a curved surface. Common everyday examples include cans, pillars, drums, and pipes.

Cylinder Formulas
CSA = 2πrh
TSA = 2πr(r + h)
Volume = πr²h
where r = base radius, h = height
💡 Hollow Cylinder (Pipe)
For a pipe with inner radius r₁ and outer radius r₂: CSA = 2πh(r₁+r₂) and Volume = πh(r₂²−r₁²).

2.3 Cone

A cone has a circular base and tapers to a single pointed top called the apex. The vertical height (h) is the perpendicular distance from the apex to the base centre, while the slant height (l) runs along the sloping surface from the apex to the base edge.

Cone Formulas
Slant Height l = √(r² + h²)
CSA = πrl
TSA = πr(r + l)
Volume = (1/3)πr²h
where r = base radius, h = vertical height, l = slant height
⚠️ h vs l — Most Common Confusion
Always check what the question provides.
• Given h → find l = √(r²+h²) before computing CSA.
• Given l → find h = √(l²−r²) before computing Volume.

2.4 Sphere

A sphere is a perfectly round solid where every point on the surface is at an equal distance from the centre. Unlike a cylinder or cone, it has no flat base — the entire surface is curved.

Sphere Formulas
Surface Area = 4πr²
Volume = (4/3)πr³
where r = radius

2.5 Hemisphere

A hemisphere is exactly half of a sphere, with one curved surface and one flat circular base. You'll encounter it in problems involving bowls, domes, and ice cream scoops on cones.

Hemisphere Formulas
CSA = 2πr²
TSA = 3πr² (= CSA + flat base = 2πr² + πr²)
Volume = (2/3)πr³
Section 03

Surface Area of Combinations of Solids

Most real-world objects are not simple solids — they are formed by joining two or more basic shapes. A toy rocket could be a cone sitting on top of a cylinder; a capsule could be a cylinder capped with two hemispheres. When two shapes are joined, their combined surface area is not simply the sum of their individual surface areas. The areas of the joined faces are now hidden inside and must be excluded.

🏆 Golden Rule — Surface Area of Combinations

Surface Area = Sum of exposed curved/lateral surfaces of each component solid only.

Do NOT count the areas at the junction — those faces are hidden inside.

  • Identify each individual solid that makes up the combination.
  • List all the surfaces of each solid separately.
  • Remove every surface that is joined to another solid (those are now internal).
  • Add up only the remaining visible, exposed surfaces.

3.1 Cone Placed on a Cylinder

Cone on Cylinder — Surface Area
TSA = CSA of Cylinder + Base of Cylinder + CSA of Cone
= 2πrh₁ + πr² + πrl
(The top circle of the cylinder and the base circle of the cone are both excluded — they are joined.)
✏️ Worked Example 3.1 — Pencil-Shaped Toy (Cone on Cylinder)

Problem: A solid toy consists of a cylinder with a conical top. The cylinder has height 12 cm and radius 3 cm. The cone has height 4 cm. Find the total surface area. (π = 3.14)

Step 1 — Slant height of cone:
l = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5 cm

Step 2 — Exposed areas:
CSA of cylinder = 2 × 3.14 × 3 × 12 = 226.08 cm²
Base of cylinder = 3.14 × 3² = 28.26 cm²
CSA of cone = 3.14 × 3 × 5 = 47.1 cm²

Answer: TSA = 226.08 + 28.26 + 47.1 = 301.44 cm²

3.2 Hemisphere on a Cylinder

Another frequent combination is a hemisphere capping the top of a cylinder — like a dome-roof water tank or a medicine capsule. The flat face of the hemisphere coincides with the top circle of the cylinder, so both are excluded.

Hemisphere on Cylinder — Surface Area
TSA = CSA of Cylinder + Base of Cylinder + CSA of Hemisphere
= 2πrh + πr² + 2πr²
= πr(2h + 3r)
✏️ Worked Example 3.2 — Medicine Capsule (Cylinder + 2 Hemispheres)

Problem: A capsule is a cylinder with a hemisphere at each end. Total length = 14 mm, diameter = 5 mm. Find the surface area. (π = 22/7)

r = 2.5 mm
Height of cylinder = 14 − 2(2.5) = 9 mm

CSA of cylinder = 2 × (22/7) × 2.5 × 9 ≈ 141.43 mm²
CSA of both hemispheres = 4πr² = 4 × (22/7) × 6.25 ≈ 78.57 mm²

Total Surface Area = 141.43 + 78.57 = 220 mm²

3.3 Hemisphere Carved from a Solid

When a hemisphere is scooped out from a solid (e.g., a hemispherical pit on top of a wooden block), the carved curved surface is now exposed and must be added, but the circular opening it creates replaces the original flat area of the top face.

Cube with Hemispherical Cavity on Top
TSA = 6a² − πr² + 2πr²
= 6a² + πr²
Section 04

Volume of Combinations of Solids

Unlike surface area, volumes of combined solids work differently — they simply add together. When two solids are joined, the total volume is the sum of both individual volumes. There is no need to subtract anything at the junction, because volume measures the space enclosed inside, not the outer surface.

However, if one shape is removed (carved out) from another, you subtract that volume.

🏆 Golden Rule — Volume of Combinations

Volume of Combination = Sum of individual volumes (nothing subtracted unless a shape is removed).

If one solid is carved out: Volume = Outer solid's volume − Carved solid's volume.

4.1 Cone Sitting on a Hemisphere

Cone on Hemisphere — Volume
Volume = Volume of Cone + Volume of Hemisphere
= (1/3)πr²h + (2/3)πr³
✏️ Worked Example 4.1 — Floating Buoy

Problem: A buoy is shaped like a cone on top of a hemisphere, both with radius 7 cm. Total height = 19 cm. Find the volume. (π = 22/7)

Height of cone = 19 − 7 = 12 cm

Volume of cone = (1/3) × (22/7) × 49 × 12 = 616 cm³
Volume of hemisphere = (2/3) × (22/7) × 343 ≈ 718.67 cm³

Total Volume ≈ 1334.67 cm³

4.2 Hemisphere Removed from a Cylinder

Cylinder minus Hemisphere — Volume
Volume = Volume of Cylinder − Volume of Hemisphere
= πr²h − (2/3)πr³
✏️ Worked Example 4.2 — Cylinder with Hemispherical Depression at Bottom

Problem: A cylindrical vessel has radius 3.5 cm and height 10 cm, with a hemispherical depression at the bottom. Find the inner surface area and volume. (π = 22/7)

Inner CSA of cylinder = 2 × (22/7) × 3.5 × 10 = 220 cm²
CSA of hemisphere = 2 × (22/7) × 12.25 = 77 cm²

Inner Surface Area = 297 cm²

Volume = (22/7) × 12.25 × 10 − (2/3) × (22/7) × 42.875
= 385 − 89.83 = 295.17 cm³

Section 05

Conversion of Solid from One Shape to Another

This concept is tested very frequently. The key idea is: when a solid is melted and recast into a different shape, the volume remains the same. No material is gained or lost in the process — only the shape changes. The same logic applies when a liquid is poured from one container into another.

🏆 The Conversion Principle

Volume of original solid = Volume of new solid(s)

If one large solid is recast into n identical smaller solids:
Volume of large solid = n × Volume of one small solid
∴ n = Volume of large solid ÷ Volume of small solid

5.1 Sphere Melted into Smaller Spheres

Number of Small Spheres
n = (4/3)πR³ ÷ (4/3)πr³ = R³ / r³
where R = radius of large sphere, r = radius of each small sphere
✏️ Worked Example 5.1

Problem: A sphere of radius 12 cm is melted into 64 smaller identical spheres. Find the radius of each.

64 × (4/3)πr³ = (4/3)π × 12³
64r³ = 1728 → r³ = 27 → r = 3 cm

5.2 Cylinder Melted into Cones

Number of Cones
n = πR²H ÷ (1/3)πr²h = 3R²H / r²h
where R, H = cylinder radius & height; r, h = cone radius & height
✏️ Worked Example 5.2

Problem: A cylinder of radius 6 cm and height 8 cm is recast into cones each of radius 2 cm and height 3 cm. How many cones?

Volume of cylinder = π × 36 × 8 = 288π cm³
Volume of one cone = (1/3) × π × 4 × 3 = 4π cm³
Number of cones = 288π / 4π = 72

5.3 Water Flow and Tank Filling Problems

Flow Rate Formula
Volume of water per second = πr² × v
Time to fill tank = Volume of tank ÷ Flow rate per second
where r = pipe radius, v = speed of water flow
✏️ Worked Example 5.3 — Pipe Filling a Tank

Problem: A pipe of internal radius 1 cm carries water at 80 cm/s. How long to fill a cylindrical tank of radius 40 cm and height 72 cm?

Volume of tank = π × 1600 × 72 = 115200π cm³
Flow per second = π × 1² × 80 = 80π cm³/s
Time = 115200π / 80π = 1440 seconds = 24 minutes

Section 06

Frustum of a Cone

A frustum is what you get when you slice off the top of a cone with a cut parallel to the base. The result is a solid with a large circular base (radius R), a smaller circular top (radius r), a vertical height (h), and a slant height (l) along the sloping surface. Everyday examples include buckets, lamp shades, flower pots, and drinking glasses.

Frustum Formulas
Slant Height l = √[h² + (R−r)²]

CSA = π(R + r)l

TSA = π(R + r)l + πR² + πr²
= π [ (R+r)l + R² + r² ]

Volume = (πh/3)(R² + Rr + r²)
where R = larger base radius, r = smaller top radius, h = height, l = slant height
🧠 Memory Aids for Frustum
• CSA resembles a trapezium area: π × (sum of the two parallel radii) × slant height.
• TSA = CSA + area of both circular ends (large base + small top).
• Volume bracket has three terms: R², Rr, r² — think "R · Rr · r".
• Slant height: always l = √[h² + (R−r)²] — never h²+R² or h²+r².
✏️ Worked Example 6.1 — Bucket (Frustum)

Problem: A frustum-shaped bucket has bottom radius 20 cm, top radius 10 cm, and height 30 cm. Find the volume and cost of metal sheet at ₹15 per 100 cm². (π = 3.14)

Slant height: l = √[30² + (20−10)²] = √[900+100] = √1000 ≈ 31.62 cm

Volume:
V = (3.14 × 30/3)(400 + 200 + 100) = 31.4 × 700 = 21,980 cm³ ≈ 21.98 litres

Metal sheet area (open top):
CSA = 3.14 × 30 × 31.62 ≈ 2980.71 cm²
Base = 3.14 × 400 = 1256 cm²
Total = 4236.71 cm²

Cost = (4236.71/100) × 15 = ₹635.51

✏️ Worked Example 6.2 — Frustum Recast into a Sphere

Problem: A metallic frustum with R = 6 cm, r = 4 cm, h = 14 cm is melted into a sphere. Find the sphere's radius. (π = 22/7)

Volume of frustum = (22/7 × 14/3)(36 + 24 + 16) = (44/3) × 76 ≈ 1114.67 cm³

(4/3)πR³ = 1114.67 → R³ ≈ 266 → R ≈ 6.43 cm

Section 07

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the errors that cost students the most marks. Study them carefully before the exam.

MistakeWhy It's WrongCorrect Approach
Adding all TSAs of individual solids Counts the joined (hidden) surfaces twice Add only exposed surfaces; exclude the joined faces
Using h instead of l in cone CSA formula CSA = πrl, not πrh Always compute l = √(r²+h²) first when l is not given
Using CSA (2πr²) instead of TSA (3πr²) for hemisphere TSA includes the flat circular base TSA of hemisphere = 2πr² + πr² = 3πr²
Subtracting volumes in combination problems Volumes add together unless a shape is carved out Volume of combination = sum of all individual volumes
Using l = √(h²+R²) for frustum slant height Ignores the difference between the two radii Always use l = √[h² + (R−r)²]
Mixing units (cm and m in the same problem) Gives completely wrong numerical answers Convert all measurements to the same unit before solving

Section 09

Key Points to Remember

  • Surface area of a combination = sum of exposed surfaces only. Joined/hidden faces are never counted.
  • Volume of a combination = sum of all individual volumes. Nothing is subtracted unless a shape is carved out.
  • Conversion problems: volume before melting = volume after recasting. This is the single most important principle.
  • Slant height must be calculated as l = √(r² + h²) whenever only the vertical height is given.
  • Frustum slant height: l = √[h² + (R−r)²] — the most commonly confused formula in this chapter.
  • For sphere-to-sphere conversion: number of small spheres = (R/r)³.
  • Hemisphere TSA = 3πr² (always includes the flat circular base area).
  • Use π = 22/7 when numbers are multiples of 7; use π = 3.14 otherwise.
  • Always draw a clearly labelled diagram — it makes it easy to identify which surfaces are visible.
  • Recall: 1 litre = 1000 cm³ — essential for converting volume to capacity in water-related problems.
Section 10

Practice Questions

These questions follow CBSE board exam patterns. Attempt all three levels. Show every step — the board awards marks for working, not just the final answer.

10.1 — 1 Mark Questions (VSA)

  1. A cone and a cylinder share the same base radius and height. What is the ratio of their volumes?
  2. A cone has slant height 10 cm and base radius 6 cm. Find its vertical height.
  3. A solid sphere of radius r is melted into 8 identical smaller spheres. Find the radius of each small sphere.
  4. Find the curved surface area of a frustum with R = 10 cm, r = 4 cm, and slant height l = 13 cm.
  5. A hemisphere of radius 7 cm has its flat face open. Write the formula for the total exposed surface area.
  6. Two cubes, each of volume 64 cm³, are joined end to end. Find the surface area of the resulting cuboid.

10.2 — 3 Mark Questions (SA)

  1. A toy consists of a cone of radius 3.5 cm mounted on a hemisphere of the same radius. Total height = 15.5 cm. Find the total surface area of the toy. (π = 22/7)
  2. A metallic sphere of radius 6 cm is melted and drawn into a wire of diameter 0.2 cm. Find the length of the wire. (π = 3.14)
  3. An inverted conical vessel (open at the top) has height 8 cm and top radius 5 cm. It is full of water. Lead shots, each a sphere of radius 0.5 cm, are dropped in until one-quarter of the water overflows. Find the number of lead shots dropped.
  4. A solid is shaped like a cone standing on a hemisphere, both with radius 1 cm and cone height 1 cm. Find the volume in terms of π.
  5. Water flows at 2.52 km/h through a cylindrical pipe of diameter 14 cm into a rectangular tank 10 m long and 4 m wide. In how many minutes will the water level rise by 3 m? (π = 22/7)

10.3 — 5 Mark Questions (LA)

  1. A tent is shaped like a cylinder topped by a cone. The cylindrical part has height 2.1 m and diameter 4 m; the conical part has slant height 2.8 m. Find (i) the canvas area required, and (ii) its cost at ₹500 per m². (π = 22/7)
  2. A frustum-shaped bucket has radii 40 cm (bottom) and 20 cm (top), and height 45 cm. Find its capacity and the cost of tin sheet used at ₹1.50 per dm². (π = 22/7)
  3. A hemispherical tank of diameter 3 m is emptied by a pipe at 3 and 4/7 litres per second. How long to empty half the tank? (π = 22/7; 1 litre = 0.001 m³)
  4. A solid cylinder of height 10 cm and base radius 3.5 cm has a hemisphere scooped out from each end. Find the total surface area of the resulting article. (π = 22/7)
  5. A solid right circular cylinder (height 10 cm, base radius 6 cm) has a cone of the same base and height removed from it. Find the volume and total surface area of the remaining solid. (π = 3.14)

🎯 Board Exam Strategy for This Chapter

  1. Memorise all formulas from the Complete Formula Summary — write them from memory every day.
  2. Always find the slant height first in any cone-related problem before touching the CSA formula.
  3. For surface area questions, sketch the shape and mark which faces are visible to the outside.
  4. For conversion questions, simply equate volumes and solve — do not overthink the setup.
  5. Show all intermediate steps — CBSE is generous with step marks even if the final answer is wrong.
  6. A wrong slant height cascades into wrong CSA and TSA — double-check this calculation always.
  7. Frustum problems often carry 5 marks — practise Example 6.1 until you can solve it without notes.
  8. Check units at the end — convert cm³ to litres when the question asks for capacity.
