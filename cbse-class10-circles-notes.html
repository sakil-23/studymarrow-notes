01 Introduction to Circles

A circle is a simple closed curve in a plane. Every point on the circle is at the same distance from a fixed interior point called the centre. The fixed distance from the centre to any point on the circle is called the radius.

In Class 10, we study the relationship between a circle and lines drawn in its plane — especially when a line touches the circle at exactly one point.

02 Key Terms and Parts of a Circle

Before studying theorems, it is important to be familiar with the standard vocabulary used in circle geometry.

TermDefinition
Centre (O)The fixed interior point equidistant from every point on the circle.
Radius (r)The distance from the centre to any point on the circle.
Diameter (d)A chord passing through the centre. d = 2r. It is the longest chord.
ChordA line segment joining any two points on the circle.
ArcA portion of the circumference. A minor arc is smaller than a semicircle; a major arc is larger.
SectorThe region enclosed between two radii and the arc between them.
SegmentThe region between a chord and the arc it cuts off.
SecantA line that intersects a circle at two distinct points.
TangentA line that touches the circle at exactly one point (the point of tangency).
Definition The circumference of a circle is the total length of its boundary, given by C = 2πr. The area enclosed is A = πr².

03 Tangent to a Circle

A tangent to a circle is a straight line in the plane of the circle that meets the circle at exactly one point. This point is called the point of tangency or the point of contact.

Unlike a secant (which cuts through the circle at two points), a tangent merely "grazes" the circle at a single location and does not enter its interior.

Remember At the point of tangency P, the tangent line is always perpendicular to the radius drawn to that point. This forms a 90° angle.

04 Theorem 1 — Tangent is Perpendicular to the Radius

Theorem 10.1 The tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius drawn to the point of tangency.

Statement

If a line l is tangent to a circle with centre O at point P, then OP ⊥ l, i.e., the angle between the radius OP and the tangent at P is 90°.

Proof (by contradiction)

Assume that the tangent l at point P is not perpendicular to the radius OP. Then, from O, we can draw another line OQ perpendicular to l (where Q is the foot of the perpendicular). In right triangle OQP, the hypotenuse OP would be longer than OQ (since the hypotenuse is always the longest side). This means Q is closer to the centre O than P, so Q would lie inside the circle.

But if Q lies inside the circle and is also a point on the tangent line l, then the line l must cross the circle at two distinct points — which contradicts our assumption that l is a tangent (touching at only one point). Therefore, our assumption is false, and the tangent must be perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact.

∴ OP ⊥ l   (Hence Proved)

Corollary Since a perpendicular from the centre to the tangent is the shortest distance, the radius to the point of tangency is the shortest line from the centre to the tangent. This also means: only one tangent can be drawn at any given point on a circle.

05 Theorem 2 — Equal Tangents from an External Point

Theorem 10.2 The lengths of the two tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal.

Statement

If two tangents PA and PB are drawn from an external point P to a circle with centre O, where A and B are the points of tangency, then PA = PB.

Proof

Consider the two right-angled triangles OAP and OBP:

  • OA = OB — both are radii of the same circle.
  • OP = OP — common hypotenuse.
  • ∠OAP = ∠OBP = 90° — radius is perpendicular to tangent (from Theorem 1).

By the RHS (Right angle – Hypotenuse – Side) congruence criterion, △OAP ≅ △OBP. Therefore, by CPCT (Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles), PA = PB.

∴ PA = PB   (Hence Proved)

Also Observe From the proof, △OAP ≅ △OBP also tells us that ∠OPA = ∠OPB — meaning OP bisects the angle ∠APB. Similarly, OP is the perpendicular bisector of chord AB.

06 Number of Tangents from a Point

The number of tangents that can be drawn from a point to a circle depends on the position of the point relative to the circle.

Position of PointNumber of TangentsReason
Inside the circle0Any line through an interior point will always intersect the circle at two points.
On the circle1Exactly one tangent can be drawn at any point on the circle.
Outside the circle2Two tangents of equal length can always be drawn from an external point.

07 Important Solved Examples

Example 1

Problem From an external point P, a tangent PT is drawn to a circle with centre O and radius 5 cm. If OP = 13 cm, find the length of the tangent PT.

Solution: Since the tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency T, triangle OTP is right-angled at T.

By Pythagoras' theorem:

PT² = OP² − OT² = 13² − 5² = 169 − 25 = 144
∴ PT = 12 cm

Example 2

Problem Two tangents PA and PB are drawn from an external point P to a circle with centre O. If ∠APB = 70°, find ∠AOB.

Solution: Since OA ⊥ PA and OB ⊥ PB (radius ⊥ tangent), the sum of angles in quadrilateral OAPB is 360°.

∠OAP + ∠OBP + ∠APB + ∠AOB = 360°
90° + 90° + 70° + ∠AOB = 360°
∠AOB = 360° − 250° = 110°

Example 3

Problem Prove that the tangents drawn at the ends of a diameter of a circle are parallel.

Solution: Let AB be a diameter of a circle with centre O. Let l be the tangent at A and m be the tangent at B.

Since the radius OA ⊥ tangent l, the angle between AB and l is 90°. Similarly, the angle between AB and m is 90°. Since both tangents make equal corresponding angles (90°) with the transversal AB, by the converse of the co-interior angles theorem, l ∥ m.

∴ Tangents at ends of a diameter are parallel.   (Proved)

Example 4

Problem A quadrilateral ABCD is drawn to circumscribe a circle. Prove that AB + CD = AD + BC.

Solution: Let the circle touch sides AB, BC, CD, DA at points P, Q, R, S respectively.

Using the equal tangents theorem from each vertex:

  • From A: AP = AS
  • From B: BP = BQ
  • From C: CQ = CR
  • From D: DR = DS

Adding all four:

AP + BP + CQ + DR = AS + BQ + CR + DS
(AP+BP) + (CQ+DR) = (AS+DS) + (BQ+CR)
AB + CD = AD + BC   (Proved)
