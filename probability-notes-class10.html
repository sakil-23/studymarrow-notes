Section 01

Introduction to Probability

Probability is the branch of mathematics concerned with measuring how likely an event is to occur. Without even realising it, we make probability-based judgements every day — checking whether it might rain, deciding whether a bus will arrive on time, or estimating the chances of winning a game. Probability replaces vague terms like "very likely" or "almost impossible" with precise numbers ranging from 0 to 1.

In Class 9, you worked with experimental (empirical) probability, where the probability of an event was calculated from the actual results of an experiment conducted many times. In Class 10, the focus shifts to theoretical probability — working out what should happen based on logic and equally likely outcomes, without performing any real experiment. Both approaches are connected, and understanding their relationship is an important idea in this chapter.

Fig 1.1 — Probability always lies between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain)
Why This Chapter Matters in Board Exams Probability is one of the most scoring chapters in Class 10 Mathematics — the formula count is small, the logic is accessible, and a well-prepared student can score full marks consistently. Board exams typically allocate 2–5 marks to this chapter through problems involving coins, dice, playing cards, and bags of coloured balls.

Section 03

Theoretical Probability

Theoretical probability (also known as classical probability) lets us calculate the likelihood of an event using pure mathematical reasoning, without conducting any experiment. It works under one key assumption: all outcomes must be equally likely. A fair coin, an unbiased die, and a well-shuffled deck of cards all satisfy this condition.

P(E) = n(E) / n(S) where  n(E) = number of outcomes favourable to event E
           n(S) = total number of outcomes in the sample space S
0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1 P(E) = 0  →  Impossible event (cannot happen)
P(E) = 1  →  Certain event (will always happen)
0 < P(E) < 1  →  Event may or may not happen
Fig 3.1 — Visual comparison of probability values for common events
WORKED EXAMPLE 3.1 — Probability with a Single Die

Problem: A fair die is rolled once. Find the probability of: (a) getting a number greater than 4, (b) getting an even number, (c) getting a number divisible by 3.

Step 1 — Sample Space: S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} → n(S) = 6

(a) Numbers greater than 4: {5, 6} → n(E) = 2 → P(E) = 2/6 = 1/3

(b) Even numbers: {2, 4, 6} → n(E) = 3 → P(E) = 3/6 = 1/2

(c) Divisible by 3: {3, 6} → n(E) = 2 → P(E) = 2/6 = 1/3

WORKED EXAMPLE 3.2 — Probability with Two Coins

Problem: Two fair coins are tossed simultaneously. Find: (a) exactly one Head, (b) at least one Tail, (c) no Heads.

Step 1 — Sample Space: S = {HH, HT, TH, TT} → n(S) = 4

(a) Exactly one Head: {HT, TH} → n = 2 → P = 2/4 = 1/2

(b) At least one Tail: {HT, TH, TT} → n = 3 → P = 3/4

(c) No Heads (both Tails): {TT} → n = 1 → P = 1/4

Section 04

Complementary Events

For any event E, the complementary event (written as E′ or "not E") includes all outcomes in the sample space that do not belong to E. Together, E and E′ cover every possible outcome — exactly one of them must occur in any trial.

P(E′) = 1 − P(E) Equivalently:   P(E) + P(E′) = 1
This identity is always true for any event E.
Fig 4.1 — E and its complement E′ together make up the entire sample space S
When to Use the Complement Method Whenever a problem uses the words at least, at most, not, or none, the complement method usually makes the calculation much easier. Instead of listing many favourable outcomes, calculate the probability of the simpler opposite case and subtract from 1.

Example: "At least 1 Head from 3 coins" → P(no heads) = 1/8 → Answer = 1 − 1/8 = 7/8
WORKED EXAMPLE 4.1 — Complementary Events with Balls

Problem: A bag holds 5 red, 3 blue, and 2 green balls. One ball is drawn at random. Find: (a) P(red), (b) P(not red), (c) P(not green).

Total balls: 5 + 3 + 2 = 10

(a) P(red) = 5/10 = 1/2

(b) P(not red) = 1 − 1/2 = 1/2   [Verify: (3+2)/10 = 5/10 = 1/2 ✓]

(c) P(green) = 2/10 = 1/5 → P(not green) = 1 − 1/5 = 4/5

WORKED EXAMPLE 5.1 — Single Card Drawn

Problem: One card is drawn at random from a well-shuffled deck of 52 cards. Find the probability of drawing: (a) a king, (b) a red card, (c) a face card, (d) the ace of spades, (e) a card that is not a face card.

n(S) = 52

(a) Kings in deck = 4 → P = 4/52 = 1/13

(b) Red cards = 26 → P = 26/52 = 1/2

(c) Face cards = 12 → P = 12/52 = 3/13

(d) Ace of spades = 1 → P = 1/52

(e) Not a face card: 52 − 12 = 40 → P = 40/52 = 10/13   [OR: 1 − 3/13 = 10/13 ✓]

WORKED EXAMPLE 5.2 — Cards with Combined Conditions

Problem: From a standard deck, find the probability of drawing: (a) a red face card, (b) a black king, (c) a queen or jack.

(a) Red face cards = J, Q, K of ♥ + J, Q, K of ♦ = 6 → P = 6/52 = 3/26

(b) Black kings = K♣ + K♠ = 2 → P = 2/52 = 1/26

(c) Queens (4) + Jacks (4) = 8 → P = 8/52 = 2/13

Section 06

Probability with Coins and Dice

Coin and die problems test your ability to correctly enumerate the sample space. The biggest source of errors is two-dice problems — students often forget that the order of outcomes matters.

6.1 Two Dice — Full Sample Space (36 outcomes)

When two dice are rolled, every outcome is an ordered pair (a, b) where a is the first die and b is the second die. Since (1,2) and (2,1) are different outcomes (different dice landed differently), the full sample space has 6 × 6 = 36 ordered pairs.

WORKED EXAMPLE 6.1 — Three Coins Tossed

Problem: Three fair coins are tossed together. Find: (a) P(exactly 2 Heads), (b) P(at least 1 Head), (c) P(at most 1 Tail).

Sample Space: S = {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT} → n(S) = 8

(a) Exactly 2 Heads: {HHT, HTH, THH} → n = 3 → P = 3/8

(b) At least 1 Head → use complement. P(no heads) = P(TTT) = 1/8 → P = 1 − 1/8 = 7/8

(c) At most 1 Tail = 0 Tails or 1 Tail: {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH} → n = 4 → P = 4/8 = 1/2

Section 07

Probability with Balls, Numbers & Other Contexts

Beyond coins, dice, and cards, CBSE exams also test probability using bags of coloured balls, numbered slips, and lottery-style setups. The underlying approach is always the same — write the total, count the favourable outcomes, apply the formula.

Key Principle for Ball Problems Treat every ball as a distinct individual, even when two balls share the same colour. The total number of balls is always n(S). Unless told otherwise, every ball is equally likely to be drawn.
WORKED EXAMPLE 7.1 — Coloured Balls in a Bag

Problem: A bag contains 3 red, 5 black, and 2 white balls. One ball is drawn at random. Find: (a) P(black), (b) P(not white), (c) P(red or black), (d) P(green).

n(S) = 3 + 5 + 2 = 10

(a) P(black) = 5/10 = 1/2

(b) P(not white) = 1 − 2/10 = 8/10 = 4/5

(c) P(red or black) = (3+5)/10 = 4/5

(d) P(green) = 0/10 = 0   [No green balls — impossible event]

WORKED EXAMPLE 7.2 — Numbered Slips in a Box

Problem: Cards numbered 1 to 25 are in a box. One is drawn at random. Find: (a) P(prime), (b) P(divisible by 5), (c) P(perfect square), (d) P(multiple of 3 or 7).

n(S) = 25

(a) Primes: {2,3,5,7,11,13,17,19,23} → n = 9 → P = 9/25

(b) Multiples of 5: {5,10,15,20,25} → n = 5 → P = 1/5

(c) Perfect squares: {1,4,9,16,25} → n = 5 → P = 1/5

(d) Multiples of 3: 8 numbers; Multiples of 7: 3 numbers; Common (21): 1 → Total = 8 + 3 − 1 = 10 → P = 10/25 = 2/5

Section 08

Experimental vs Theoretical Probability

Understanding the difference between these two approaches — and how they relate — is a conceptual topic that can appear as a short-answer question in board exams.

FeatureExperimental (Empirical)Theoretical (Classical)
DefinitionDerived from the results of an actual experimentDerived from mathematical reasoning about equally likely outcomes
FormulaP(E) = (times E occurred) / (total trials)P(E) = n(E) / n(S)
ConsistencyVaries from one set of trials to the nextFixed — always gives the same answer
ExampleTossing a coin 100 times → 48 Heads → P ≈ 0.48P(Head) = 1/2 = 0.5 (always)
Use caseWhen outcomes are not equally likely, or a real experiment is possibleWhen outcomes are equally likely and the experiment is ideal
The Law of Large Numbers As the number of trials in an experiment grows larger and larger, the experimental probability gets progressively closer to the theoretical probability. For example, tossing a coin 10 times might give 4 Heads (P = 0.4), but tossing it 10,000 times will yield a result very close to 5,000 Heads (P ≈ 0.5). This is why theoretical probability is described as the "long-run" prediction.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the most frequently seen errors in CBSE board answer scripts for this chapter. Each one has directly cost students marks in real examinations.

MistakeWhat Goes WrongCorrect Approach
Wrong sample space for 2 coins Writing {HH, HT, TT} — the outcome TH is missed entirely Full space: {HH, HT, TH, TT} — always 4 outcomes
Ace counted as a face card Treating face cards as 13 (adding Ace) instead of 12 Face cards = Jack, Queen, King only = 12 total
Fraction not simplified Leaving answer as 6/36 without reducing Always simplify: 6/36 = 1/6
Formula inverted Dividing n(S) by n(E) instead of n(E) by n(S) P = n(E) / n(S) — favourable over total, always
(1,2) and (2,1) treated as the same Undercounting two-dice outcomes by ignoring order Both are distinct ordered pairs — both must be counted
Complement formula error Writing P(not E) = 1 + P(E) instead of subtracting P(not E) = 1 − P(E)   [subtract, not add]
Impossible event given a non-zero value Attempting to calculate instead of recognising P = 0 State directly: P(impossible event) = 0

Section 12

Practice Questions

These questions follow actual CBSE board exam patterns. Before calculating, always write the sample space explicitly. Show your formula, substitution, and simplified answer — examiners award step marks even when the final answer carries an arithmetic error.

1-Mark Questions (VSA)

  1. A die is thrown once. Find the probability of getting a prime number.
  2. A card is drawn from a standard deck of 52. Find P(a king).
  3. Write the probability of an impossible event.
  4. If P(E) = 0.37, what is P(not E)?
  5. Two coins are tossed. Write the complete sample space.
  6. A bag has 4 red and 6 blue balls. Find P(drawing a blue ball).
  7. What is the probability of getting a number greater than 6 on a standard die?

3-Mark Questions (SA)

  1. A bag contains 5 red, 4 black, and 3 white balls. Find: (a) P(red), (b) P(not black), (c) P(white or red).
  2. Cards numbered 1 to 20 are placed in a box. A card is drawn at random. Find: (a) P(even number), (b) P(prime number), (c) P(divisible by 4).
  3. Two dice are rolled simultaneously. Find: (a) P(sum = 8), (b) P(doublet), (c) P(sum < 5).
  4. A card is drawn from a standard deck. Find: (a) P(ace of hearts), (b) P(red face card), (c) P(not a face card).
  5. Three coins are tossed. Find: (a) P(exactly one Head), (b) P(at least two Heads), (c) P(at most one Tail).

5-Mark Questions (LA)

  1. A bag contains 15 white and some black balls. The probability of drawing a black ball is twice that of drawing a white ball. Find the number of black balls and P(drawing a white ball).
  2. Two dice are thrown together. Find the probability of: (a) same number on both dice, (b) sum ≥ 10, (c) sum is a prime number, (d) sum ≤ 6.
  3. From a pack of 52 cards, a card is drawn at random. Find P: (a) face card, (b) red card that is not a face card, (c) a spade, (d) a heart card showing a number between 2 and 9 inclusive.
  4. Twelve defective pens are accidentally mixed with 132 good ones. One pen is picked at random. Find: (a) P(good pen), (b) P(defective pen). What does P(good) + P(defective) equal, and why?
Board Exam Strategy for Probability
  1. Always write the sample space first — never skip it, even when it seems obvious.
  2. For two coins: write all 4 outcomes. For two dice: remember 36 ordered pairs.
  3. Face cards = 12 only (J, Q, K). Ace is NOT a face card. Memorise the full deck structure.
  4. Use the complement method whenever you see "at least," "at most," or "not."
  5. Always reduce your final probability fraction to its lowest terms.
  6. Verify answers using P(E) + P(E′) = 1.
  7. Write the formula, show the substitution, then give the simplified answer — this earns full step marks.
  8. Probability is one of the most reliable chapters for scoring full marks — treat it as a guaranteed 5 marks.
